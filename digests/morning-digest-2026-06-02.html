🌟 Deep Dive · 25 min
Anchored Policy Optimization: Mitigating Exploration Collapse via Support-Constrained Rectification
arXiv · Feb 11, 2026 · Promoted from bookmarks·~25 min read
Off the bookmark shelf at last. The paper’s problem statement: RLVR with group-relative advantages (GRPO and descendants) reliably suffers from exploration collapse — after a few hundred steps of training, the policy concentrates on a narrow region of behavior space, group-relative variance crashes, and the gradient signal degenerates into noise. Their diagnosis is that the collapse mechanism is not entropy reduction per se (entropy stays moderate); it is support contraction — the policy stops generating samples from regions it was once willing to explore, even when those regions remain on-distribution. The mechanism, “Anchored PO,” introduces a learned anchor distribution — a frozen snapshot of the policy taken at a configurable point in early training, after the policy has acquired competence but before collapse begins — and adds a support-constrained rectification term to the gradient update that penalizes any move that pushes probability mass away from the anchor’s support, while still allowing the policy to reweight within the anchor’s support. The result is that exploration collapse is mitigated without sacrificing the convergence of GRPO. Three things to take from a careful reading. (1) The mechanism axis for the LIFR draft. Anchored PO is the third exploration-control mechanism in the SAGE / AEM / Anchored-PO trio, and it operates at a distinct geometric level. SAGE controls exploration by reweighting samples within a batch (sample-space geometry). AEM controls exploration by modulating the policy’s entropy at decision points (output-distribution geometry). Anchored PO controls exploration by constraining the policy’s support via a learned anchor (parameter-space geometry, indirectly). Three mechanisms, three geometric instantiations — the structural symmetry with the DelTA / GCPO / EP-GRPO discriminator-view trio is striking and the LIFR draft can present these as two parallel taxonomies (credit-assignment geometry and exploration-control geometry) both unified by “the right unit of intervention should match the right unit of decision-making.” (2) The choice of anchor. The paper is honest that the anchor-selection step is the empirically tricky part: too early and the anchor encodes incompetent behavior; too late and the anchor is already in the collapse regime. They sweep anchor-timing across MATH and AIME-2024 and report that the “competence inflection point” — the step at which group-relative variance is at its peak before it starts decaying — is the best empirical heuristic. The interesting transfer for formal-methods is that the analogous anchor in a Lean-RLVR setting is the policy at the step where the agent first solves a non-trivial Mathlib lemma without backtracking. Add this to the experimental-protocol backlog. (3) The composition test with the discriminator view. Anchored PO and GCPO are operating at orthogonal axes — one constrains exploration, the other reweights credit assignment — and there is no methodological obstacle to running them simultaneously. The paper does not test this, but the LIFR draft’s “unification claim” is testable: Anchored-PO + GCPO should compose multiplicatively if the two axes are truly independent, additively if they overlap, and sub-linearly if they interfere. That is a Figure-3-quality experiment and the LIFR draft should sketch the experimental design today. (4) The connection to the contrarian Sparse Policy Selection paper. Sparse Policy Selection argues RL is “policy selection over the base model’s skill set, not capability learning.” If that is true, then exploration collapse is just the inevitable consequence of selecting too aggressively — and Anchored PO is, by their lights, a regularizer that slows the selection rate rather than a mechanism that introduces new capability. Worth a paragraph in the LIFR draft’s related-work section. Action items: (i) commit the “two parallel taxonomies” framing to the LIFR draft today; (ii) add the Anchored-PO + GCPO composition experiment to the protocol backlog as Figure 3; (iii) sketch the Lean-anchor-selection heuristic (first non-trivial Mathlib lemma without backtracking) into the formal-methods application section.

⚡ Quick Reads · 15 min total
GRPO-VPS: Enhancing Group Relative Policy Optimization with Verifiable Process Supervision for Effective Reasoning
arXiv · Apr 30, 2026·~4 min read
The formal-methods counterpart to today’s discriminator-view thread. The paper’s claim: GRPO eliminates the need for a critic model but suffers from indiscriminate credit assignment for intermediate steps — reasoning chains are scored only at the final answer, and the model gets no signal about which intermediate step caused the failure. Their mechanism, GRPO-VPS, replaces sample-level outcome scoring with verifiable process supervision: each intermediate reasoning step is checked against a procedural verifier (a constraint checker for math, a syntax/type checker for code, a Lean elaborator for theorem proving), and the per-step verification result is folded into the per-step advantage signal. They report up to 2.6-point accuracy gains and 13.7% length reductions on math benchmarks. The interesting structural point for the LIFR draft: GRPO-VPS is what the discriminator-view trio (DelTA / GCPO / EP-GRPO) looks like when the “discriminator” is replaced by an external symbolic verifier rather than a learned signal. The four mechanisms now form a cleaner taxonomy: learned discriminator (DelTA), prompt-contrastive discriminator (GCPO), self-referential entropy discriminator (EP-GRPO), and external symbolic verifier (GRPO-VPS). The LIFR draft’s unification section should present these as four points on the “source-of-the-credit-signal” axis, with the formal-methods case (Lean elaborator as verifier) as the cleanest instantiation because the verifier is provably sound by construction.
Formal Methods RLVR Trending Read
CM2: Reinforcement Learning with Checklist Rewards for Multi-Turn and Multi-Step Agentic Tool Use
arXiv · Feb 20, 2026·~4 min read
The AGENTS analogue of segment-level supervision. The problem: realistic agentic objectives (book a flight under constraints, debug a Python repo, draft a multi-step email thread) do not have a clean verifiable outcome signal — they are instead defined by a checklist of intended behaviors. CM2’s mechanism: decompose each turn’s objective into a fine-grained checklist of binary criteria with explicit evidence grounding (the model must point at the trace location that satisfies each criterion), score each criterion separately, and use the resulting dense-criterion vector as the reward signal. They train an 8B model on 8k examples and report +8 / +10 / +12 points over the SFT counterpart on three benchmarks (τ²-Bench, BFCL-V4, ToolSandbox). The interesting connection for the LIFR draft: CM2’s checklist rewards are sparse-assignment / dense-criteria, the dual of GRPO-VPS’s dense-assignment / sparse-criteria. Both are searching for the right granularity at which to inject feedback, but they make opposite design choices about where the granularity lives (in the assignment vs. in the criteria). For the AGENTS chapter of the dissertation, this is a clean pedagogical pair — same problem, dual solutions.
