🌟 Deep Dive · 25 min
Re²: Unlocking LLM Reasoning via Reinforcement Learning with Re-solving
arXiv · Mar 7, 2026 · Promoted from bookmarks·~25 min read
Off the shelf at last — Thursday’s digest sent it to “next week,” but the weekend is the better slot precisely because this is the deep dive that earns its 25 minutes by testing the LIFR draft rather than just adding a row to it. Re²’s core idea is simple and, for that reason, dangerous to the three-axis story: when the policy fails a problem, instead of (or in addition to) reshaping the advantage or the exploration temperature, Re² restarts the solve — it conditions a fresh attempt on a compressed trace of the failed one and rewards the policy for re-deriving a correct path from that warm-but-not-anchored state. The mechanism is a learned restart heuristic: the model learns when a partial trajectory is unsalvageable and a re-solve is cheaper than continuing, and it learns what to carry across the restart boundary. The reason this is the right adversarial read for today. Thursday’s draft change committed to a third axis — reward-schedule geometry — whose canonical instance is T2T’s thicken-on-failure / thin-on-success schedule. Re² also keys off failure, and the obvious lazy reading is “re-solve-on-failure is just thicken-on-failure with a hard reset.” A careful reading says that is wrong, and the difference is the whole point. T2T modifies the shape of the reward within a single trajectory’s lifetime — it is still one continuous rollout, scored differently depending on regime. Re² modifies the trajectory structure itself — it segments a rollout into pre-restart and post-restart sub-trajectories and changes the credit boundary between them. That is not a reward-schedule operation; it is a trajectory-topology operation. Three things to take from the read. (1) Re² is most likely a fourth axis, not a point on the third. The cleanest way to see it: T2T composes with a fixed rollout structure (you can thicken-then-thin any single trajectory), whereas Re² changes what counts as “a trajectory” before any reward schedule is applied. They sit at different levels of the pipeline — restart is upstream of reward shaping. If that holds under scrutiny, the LIFR grid is now credit-assignment × exploration-control × reward-schedule × trajectory-topology, and the honest move is to admit the fourth factor two days after committing the third rather than forcing Re² into a box it doesn’t fit. The composition backlog grows, but the decomposition is cleaner. (2) The Lean transfer is unusually direct — cleaner than T2T’s. In formal theorem proving, “re-solve on failure” is already a native operation: when a tactic search dead-ends, the prover backtracks to the last open goal and re-attempts with a different tactic. Re²’s learned restart heuristic is, in the Lean setting, a learned backtracking controller — learn which subgoal to backtrack to and what proof-state context to carry across. The elaborator gives a noise-free failure signal (the tactic either closes the goal or it doesn’t), so the “is this trajectory unsalvageable?” classifier that Re² must learn from noisy math rewards is handed to the Lean agent for free. This is a stronger formal-methods hook than the T2T phase-transition heuristic from Thursday, and it belongs in the same section of the draft. (3) The Sparse Policy Selection test. Re²’s gains, like T2T’s, are real but bounded, which keeps the “RL is selection within the base model’s skill set” critique alive: a restart that re-derives a correct path is, arguably, still finding a path that was already in the support, just reached by a different route. The interesting empirical question — and the one the LIFR draft should pose explicitly — is whether re-solving ever reaches a correct answer the model cannot reach in a single uninterrupted rollout. If yes, that is the first axis in the grid with a plausible claim to pushing the support boundary outward, and it deserves a dedicated probe. Action items: (i) write the verdict into the LIFR draft today — Re² as a fourth (trajectory-topology) axis, with the level-of-the-pipeline argument for why it is not a point on the reward-schedule axis; (ii) add the Lean learned-backtracking-controller note to the formal-methods application section as the cleanest of the three transfer hooks so far; (iii) add the “does re-solving exceed single-rollout reach?” probe to the experimental backlog as the one experiment that could falsify the Sparse Policy Selection frame.

Policy-Conditioned Counterfactual Credit for Verifiable RL of Long-Horizon Language Agents (CVT-RL)
arXiv · Jun 5, 2026·~4 min read
Fresh, and a genuinely new point on the credit-assignment-geometry axis — the sixth, after DelTA, GCPO, EP-GRPO, GRPO-VPS, and PROGRS. The targets are the long-horizon-agent pathologies the draft has been circling: unsupported evidence chains, belief drift, and shortcut actions. The mechanism, CVT-RL, is the first on the taxonomy to use an intervention-based credit signal: instead of inferring a step’s contribution from a learned discriminator, it estimates each step’s counterfactual contribution by controlled interventions — deleting or semantically substituting a step and measuring the change in verifiable outcome — gated by an intervention-validity check so only well-formed counterfactuals count. This is methodologically distinct from the other five points: they all answer “how good was this step?” with a model; CVT-RL answers it with an experiment. The Lean transfer is appealing — deleting a tactic and re-running the elaborator is a perfectly valid, noise-free counterfactual, so the intervention-validity gate is trivially satisfied in the formal setting. Add as the sixth row, and flag it as the credit-assignment axis’s natural pairing with Re²: counterfactual credit tells you which step to blame; re-solving tells you what to do about it.
RLVR Agents Trending Read
Reinforcement Learning from Rich Feedback with Distributional DAgger
arXiv · Jun 5, 2026·~4 min read
Fresh, and it pushes on the assumption underneath the whole RLVR literature: the one-bit reward. The paper’s framing is that sampling many responses and rewarding each with a single correct/incorrect bit throws away almost all of the signal available — execution traces, tool outputs, intermediate expert corrections. Their distributional variant of DAgger uses that richer feedback to produce a forward cross-entropy objective that does credit assignment by propagating future expert-student disagreement back to earlier decisions. The structural connection to the draft: this is a different answer to the same question PROGRS and CVT-RL ask — where does the dense signal come from? — but it sources the signal from imitation against a richer target rather than from a learned PRM or a counterfactual intervention. It is the closest thing in the recent wave to an honest hybrid of RL and supervised credit assignment, which makes it the natural baseline to position the verifier-checked methods against: when a symbolic verifier is available (Lean), do you still need the rich-feedback DAgger machinery, or does the elaborator subsume it? Worth a sentence in the related-work section as the imitation-flavored corner of the credit-signal taxonomy.
RLVR Trending Read
LeanMarathon: Toward Reliable AI Co-Mathematicians through Long-Horizon Lean Autoformalization
arXiv · Jun 5, 2026·~4 min read
The fresh formal-methods anchor for today, and the benchmark the Lean-RLVR chapter has quietly been missing. Most theorem-proving evaluation is lemma-scale — prove this one statement — which is exactly the regime where single-rollout RLVR looks best and the hard problem (compounding error across a long formalization) never shows up. LeanMarathon reframes the target as long-horizon, whole-result autoformalization: translate and verify an entire mathematical result, where reliability is dominated by error accumulation across many dependent steps rather than the difficulty of any single one. That reframing matters for the dissertation in a specific way — it is the natural evaluation home for both Re²’s learned backtracking and CVT-RL’s counterfactual credit, because both are long-horizon mechanisms that a lemma-scale benchmark cannot distinguish from vanilla GRPO. If the Lean-RLVR contribution is going to claim it improves reliability rather than peak capability, LeanMarathon (or a benchmark like it) is where that claim has to be made or broken. Add to the experimental-protocol backlog as the long-horizon evaluation target; it pairs the formal-methods chapter’s reliability argument with a benchmark that actually stresses it.
Formal Methods Trending Read
Knowledge-to-Verification (K2V): Exploring RLVR for LLMs in Knowledge-Intensive Domains
arXiv · May 18, 2026 · Promoted from bookmarks·~3 min read
Thursday slated this for Friday; the no-digest Friday slipped it to today, which is fine — it reads in three minutes and clarifies the one axis the formal-methods framing keeps eliding. K2V asks what RLVR does when the verifier is not a symbolic oracle: in knowledge-intensive domains, “correct” is a judgment about factual grounding, not a type-check, so the verifiable-reward signal has to be constructed rather than read off an elaborator. The value for the draft is as the contrast class for the Lean argument: it makes precise what the formal setting buys you (a free, sound, noise-free verifier) by showing exactly how much machinery K2V has to build to approximate that in a domain where no oracle exists. Read it as the boundary marker on the “source-of-the-verifier” axis — symbolic oracle (Lean) at one end, constructed knowledge-verifier (K2V) at the other — and use it to keep the dissertation honest about the scope of the Lean claim.
