International AI Safety Report 2026: Executive Summary

TRENDING
Yoshua Bengio et al. / 100+ AI Experts ~5 min Published Feb 2026

The world's largest collaborative AI safety assessment, led by Turing Award winner Yoshua Bengio. Key findings: inference-time scaling keeps improving models even after training ends; agent reliability is lagging badly behind capability (85% best-case reliability on key benchmarks); and no combination of current methods fully eliminates failures. The formal methods angle: the report specifically flags verification and safe RL as two of six pillars for ML certification. Your research area has regulatory tailwind.

Directly relevant to formal verification + agent safety. The 82%/14.4% executive confidence vs. actual security approval gap is a striking stat for any future paper motivation section.

DeepSeek-R1: RL Incentivizes Reasoning in LLMs — Now in Nature

TRENDING
DeepSeek-AI / Nature Vol. 645 ~4 min (abstract + intro) Peer-reviewed milestone

DeepSeek-R1 is now formally peer-reviewed and published in Nature — a landmark for RL-trained reasoning models. The core finding: pure RL (no human-labeled reasoning trajectories, no SFT warmup) causes reasoning behaviors — self-reflection, verification, dynamic strategy adaptation — to emerge naturally from the reward signal alone. The GRPO framework rewards only final correctness, not intermediate steps. This is the empirical twin of your formal verification work: if RL can self-certify reasoning, what does that mean for provable guarantees?

Read this alongside AlphaProof (Day 3). Together they bookend the RL-for-reasoning landscape: one uses formal provers as the verifier, the other uses outcome rewards alone. Your research lives in the space between them.

Lossy Self-Improvement — The Skeptic's Take on Recursive AI Progress

TRENDING
Nathan Lambert / Interconnects AI ~5 min Research commentary

Lambert argues that the recursive self-improvement story — AI training AI, compounding to infinity — hits a friction brake he calls lossy self-improvement (LSI): the more compute and agents you throw at a problem, the more redundancy and signal degradation you get. Models become central to the development loop, but they can't fully close it. This is a useful intellectual counterweight to the Karpathy autoresearch optimism from earlier in the week. Not doom — just a complexity brake on the rate of returns.

Good epistemic hygiene: always read the smart skeptic after you've read the optimist. Lambert knows post-training better than almost anyone. Read this after finishing the Naval deep dive.

Karpathy's Next Vision: SETI@home-Style Distributed AI Research

TRENDING
NextBigFuture / Andrej Karpathy ~4 min March 2026

After autoresearch ran 700 overnight experiments with one GPU, Karpathy's next proposal is to make it massively asynchronous and distributed — like SETI@home, but for AI research. Instead of emulating one PhD student, distribute the agent loop across thousands of nodes, each running independent experiment branches. Shopify's CEO already reported a 19% performance gain after 37 overnight runs using the framework on internal data. This is the logical extension of "humans are the bottleneck" — and it has direct implications for how RL hyperparameter search will be done at scale.

Complements Lambert's LSI skepticism: Karpathy sees parallelism as the answer to the compute ceiling; Lambert says the bottleneck is signal quality, not throughput. Both can be right.
