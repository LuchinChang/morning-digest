Learning-Infused Formal Reasoning (LIFR): Contract Synthesis, Artifact Reuse, and Semantic Foundations
arXiv (VERIFAI 2026 short version) · April 27, 2026·~25 min read
Read this as a map of your own research, not as a paper with results. The authors’ thesis is the one you’ve been quietly assembling all spring: formal verification has historically been an isolated activity — a one-program, one-proof effort — and the bottleneck to scaling it isn’t the prover, it’s the absence of any cumulative, reusable layer of specifications, contracts, and proof artifacts. LIFR proposes three concrete research directions, and the cleanest framing is to ask which one each paper you read this month belongs to. Direction 1: automated contract synthesis from natural language requirements. An LLM generates candidate logical predicates from informal text; an SMT solver or theorem prover then checks satisfiability and consistency; iterative feedback refines the contracts until the verifier accepts them. This is the exact loop that CktFormalizer (your May 13 deep dive) instantiated for hardware verification, and the exact loop that FormalRewardBench (your May 14 deep dive) is trying to evaluate when the “feedback” is a learned reward instead of an SMT solver. Direction 2: graph-based semantic reuse of verification artifacts across projects. This is the leg you’ve done the least reading on, and it’s also the most defensible “novel contribution” quadrant for a dissertation — the literature on artifact reuse in verification is unfashionably small, and matching/retrieval is having a renaissance via embeddings. Direction 3: rigorous semantic foundations using Unifying Theories of Programming (Hoare & He) and the Theory of Institutions (Goguen & Burstall). This is the “why does any of this compose?” leg, and it’s the one most likely to feel slow when you read it but most likely to be load-bearing in the proposal. Three concrete actions after you read it. (1) Tag every paper in your reading log this month with which LIFR direction it belongs to — this is a free taxonomy for your literature review chapter. (2) Notice that the RLVR-for-Lean papers (FormalRewardBench, ScaleLogic, NPG-as-DSPI) are all in Direction 1 — that’s a coverage gap in your reading, not an editorial choice. (3) The Theory-of-Institutions framing in Direction 3 is the right answer to the “why can your method work on both Lean and Coq?” question you’ll be asked at your prelim; sketch a one-paragraph response on the back of this page when you’re done reading.

⚡ Quick Reads · 15 min total
DGPO: Distribution-Guided Policy Optimization for Fine-Grained Credit Assignment
arXiv · May 5, 2026 (v2 May 8)·~5 min read (skim)
Two clean ideas worth pocketing. First, GRPO’s sequence-level advantage is too coarse: when a 4,000-token CoT gets a single +1, every token gets the same gradient, and the “which step actually mattered” signal is washed out. DGPO replaces the sequence-level advantage with a token-level distribution deviation signal — the policy’s deviation from a reference distribution measured pointwise, used as guidance rather than penalty. Second — and this is the part to remember — they replace the KL divergence with the Hellinger distance. KL is unbounded; under aggressive policy updates it can spike and induce gradient explosion or mode-seeking collapse. Hellinger is bounded in [0,1], so the same “distance from reference” signal becomes a safe quantitative guide instead of a destabilizing penalty. Pair this with yesterday’s NPG-as-DSPI: if NPG converges to a doubly-smoothed Bellman fixed point and GRPO is NPG-in-disguise, then DGPO is best read as “swap one of the two smoothings (the KL one) for a bounded surrogate”. Critic-free, drop-in, and easy to ablate.
RLVR Trending Read
AlphaGRPO: Unlocking Self-Reflective Multimodal Generation via Decompositional Verifiable Reward
arXiv · May 13, 2026·~4 min read (skim)
The reason this is in your digest even though it’s a multimodal-generation paper: the Decompositional Verifiable Reward (DVReward) construction is exactly the move you should be considering for partial-proof rewards in Lean. The recipe: an LLM decomposes a complex user request into atomic, verifiable semantic and quality questions (“does the image contain a red cube?”, “is the cube on the left of the sphere?”); a general MLLM then evaluates each atom and returns an interpretable, structured reward. The analogy to your domain writes itself — replace “atomic visual questions” with “atomic proof obligations” (does this lemma type-check? does the goal state contain the desired hypothesis? is the induction variable fresh?), and DVReward becomes a recipe for a dense, decomposable, verifier-grounded reward signal for theorem proving. Skim Section 3 of the paper for the prompt structure they use for decomposition.

OPSD Compresses What RLVR Teaches: A Post-RL Compaction Stage for Reasoning Models
arXiv · May 7, 2026·~3 min read (skim)
The empirical observation is the part to take away: after RLVR, you can distill the RL−trained policy back into a much smaller (and shorter-CoT) student model and recover most of the reasoning gain — suggesting most of what RLVR actually “learned” was a small set of high-leverage decision rules, not a broad capability shift. Place this next to yesterday’s ReasonMaxxer (only 1–3% of token positions are materially changed by RL) and the picture becomes coherent: RLVR is doing a small amount of work in a small number of places, and the rest is the base model. The methodological consequence is the part that matters for your work: if RLVR’s contribution is compressible, then the FormalRewardBench−style reward model only needs to be accurate at the small set of decision points where the policy actually changes — which lowers the bar for “good enough” reward modeling considerably.
Promoted RLVR Read
Simon Willison — Release: llm 0.32a2 & the migration to /v1/responses
simonwillison.net · May 12, 2026·~3 min read
OpenAI’s reasoning-capable models have quietly moved off /v1/chat/completions and onto /v1/responses, which exposes interleaved reasoning across tool calls — the model can think, call a tool, think more, call another tool, and the summarized reasoning tokens are visible to the caller throughout. Simon’s post is a release-note for his llm CLI, but the more interesting paragraph is the side observation that the new endpoint shape is essentially a streaming “reasoning-aware” protocol — the same shape an academic RL researcher would want for collecting trajectories with intermediate reward signals. The connection to today’s thread: if the API itself surfaces a fine-grained record of where the model thought, decided, and acted, then the dense-reward / fine-grained-credit assignment problem moves from “we have to infer where the work happened” to “the trace tells us”. Worth noting for your tooling.

APMPO: Adaptive Power-Mean Policy Optimization for Improved LLM Reasoning ~12 min
dGRPO: Distilled GRPO — On-Policy Optimization + Distillation for Long-Context Reasoning ~15 min
Nonsense Helps: Prompt-Space Perturbation Broadens Reasoning Exploration (the “zero-advantage problem” paper) ~10 min

Today’s saves are all on the GRPO-improvement edge of the week’s thread. APMPO replaces GRPO’s static aggregation with an adaptive power-mean over rollouts — one knob that interpolates between min, mean, and max, which lets you trade exploration for exploitation at the advantage-aggregation step. dGRPO is the long-context variant you’ll want when you start running GRPO over Lean proofs that are themselves 4k+ tokens; the trick is augmenting the GRPO update with dense guidance from a stronger teacher (a distillation-RL hybrid). Nonsense Helps is the paper to keep on your desk — it gives you a one-line fix for the zero-advantage / failure-mode-collapse problem that every RLVR-for-Lean pipeline runs into: when every rollout fails, perturb the prompt with arbitrary noise to broaden the exploration distribution. Counter-intuitive and almost certainly cheap to try.
