🔬 Deep Dive · 25 min
Type-Checked Compliance: Deterministic Guardrails for Agentic Financial Systems Using Lean 4 Theorem Proving
arXiv · Apr 1, 2026 · Lean 4 · dependent types · agentic guardrails · ~25 min
The pitch is sharper than the title suggests: express the compliance policy as a Lean 4 type, then represent every action the agent can take as a term that must inhabit that type. If the term doesn't typecheck, the action is impossible — not "rejected at runtime by a guardrail," but literally not constructible. The authors instantiate this on a regulated-finance setting (KYC, position limits, sanctions screening) where the rules are codified by humans into Lean signatures, and the LLM's job becomes writing a Lean term rather than emitting a tool-call JSON. They show the elaborator's error messages function as a high-quality reward signal for an iterative LLM loop — the model is forced to converge on a compliant action because nothing else compiles. Three notes for your own write-up: (i) this is the cleanest 2026 instance of the "shield-as-type" reading of safe RL — it's what you'd get if you took the safe-gain-scheduling library trick and replaced "finite library of pre-certified controllers" with "infinite family of programs that inhabit a refinement type"; (ii) the elaborator-as-verifier loop is structurally identical to the Frama-C/SMT loop in Broken by Default, but with much higher specification expressiveness — useful for the section of your proposal contrasting verifier APIs; (iii) the failure-mode taxonomy (Section 4) is the most reusable artifact — it gives you a vocabulary for talking about why a verifier rejects a candidate, which you'll want when designing the dense reward signal. Pair this with the Solver-Aided Policy Compliance paper from Apr 24 as a two-source baseline.

Mythos and the Unverified Cage: Z3-Based Pre-Deployment Verification for Frontier-Model Sandbox Infrastructure
arXiv · Apr 22, 2026 · Dominik Blain · Z3 · CWE-190/191/195 · sandbox security · ~4 min
The formal-methods response to last week's Mythos sandbox-escape news. The author is careful: Anthropic hasn't disclosed the actual escape vector, so the paper analyzes the vulnerability class (CWE-190/191/195 integer arithmetic) rather than the specific incident. The contribution is COBALT, a Z3-backed pre-deployment verifier that emits SAT verdicts (with witnesses) or UNSAT guarantees under explicit safety bounds for arithmetic-overflow patterns in C/C++ infrastructure. Validated on NASA cFE, wolfSSL, Eclipse Mosquitto, and NASA F Prime. Most directly useful for you: it's a fully worked-out example of "verifier as gating step before a model touches a system," which is the topology you need to argue for in the shielded-codegen proposal — and it's a citeable instance of formal methods crossing into the sandbox/runtime layer rather than the model layer.
Trending Formal Methods Code Read
Outcome Rewards Do Not Guarantee Verifiable or Causally Important Reasoning
arXiv · Apr 2026 · RLVR · Qwen2.5 · CIR / SR metrics · ~4 min
A pointed critique of the standard RLVR story. The authors introduce two metrics — Causal Importance of Reasoning (do the chain-of-thought tokens actually affect the answer?) and Sufficiency of Reasoning (can a verifier recover the answer from the CoT alone?) — and show that pure outcome-reward RLVR improves task accuracy without reliably improving either. Two fixes that work: (i) a small SFT warm-up before RLVR, and (ii) auxiliary CIR/SR rewards added on top of the outcome signal — which matches RLVR's accuracy and produces causally important, sufficient reasoning. For your proposal: this is the empirical hook you need for the "verifier-discharge rate is a better reward than answer correctness" argument. The CIR/SR metrics also generalize cleanly from math reasoning to your formal-spec setting — "did the proof obligation actually use the spec assertion?" is just CIR with the assertion as the intervention variable.
RLVR Formal Methods Read
Rethinking Token-Level Credit Assignment in RLVR: A Polarity-Entropy Analysis
arXiv · Apr 13, 2026 · HKUST(GZ) + Huawei · CMI bound · four-quadrant decomposition · ~4 min
Sparse outcome rewards make credit assignment hard. The authors slice each token update along two axes — polarity (positive/negative advantage) and entropy (under the policy) — and run a controlled ablation: reasoning improvements concentrate almost entirely in the high-entropy quadrants. They then adapt Conditional Mutual Information to the autoregressive RLVR setting and prove the credit a token can carry is upper-bounded by its entropy. The practical lesson if you're designing a verifier-in-the-loop reward: don't spread the reward signal uniformly over the chain — gate it on token entropy, since low-entropy tokens (operators, syntax) can't carry credit by construction. Pair with the Outcome-Rewards-≠-Reasoning paper above; together they give you the "what's broken" + "where the fix has to land" pair.

GPT-5.5 prompting guide — Simon Willison's notes on the official OpenAI advice
simonwillison.net · Apr 25, 2026 · prompting · API · GPT-5.5 · ~3 min
Quick read: OpenAI's two big recommendations for GPT-5.5 are (1) start prompts from scratch rather than trusting that prompts tuned for 5.2 / 5.0 will continue to work — the model has different priors about what counts as "follow the instructions" — and (2) for any tool-calling or thinking-heavy flow, emit a short user-visible acknowledgement before the first tool call so the user isn't staring at a blank screen. Less obvious takeaway for your work: this is a slow drift toward "the prompt is part of the program, not part of the data," which lines up with the policy-as-types reading from today's deep dive — the prompt becomes the place where you declare the action space, and the model's job is to inhabit it.
