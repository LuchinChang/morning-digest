🌟 Deep Dive · 25 min
SAGE: Shaping Anchors for Guided Exploration in RLVR of LLMs
arXiv · May 23, 2026·~25 min read
The cleanest new framing on the exploration problem in months. Where most of this season’s exploration work (UCPO, GCPO, EP-GRPO, Boltzmann Projection) modifies the policy-side machinery — advantage normalization, group structure, entropy bonuses — SAGE makes a structural observation that reframes the whole conversation: in reverse-KL-regularized RLVR, the anchor distribution π_ref is itself a free design choice, not a fixed object to be relaxed away from. Three pieces worth slow reading. (1) The structural argument. Sections 2–3 walk through why anchor choice is load-bearing. Standard RLVR sets π_ref = base model and adds β·KL(π ‖ π_ref) as a stability regularizer. Under reverse KL, this anchors the policy near the base — which stabilizes training but suppresses any reasoning mode the base model assigns low probability to. The paper’s key move: treat π_ref as a target to be sculpted, not a constraint to be relaxed. If you replace the base anchor with a constructed one that puts more mass on under-explored solution patterns (lower-frequency reasoning skeletons, longer chains, abstention behaviors), the same reverse-KL term that used to suppress them now pulls the policy toward them. Same regularizer, opposite effect. (2) How they construct the anchor. Section 4 is the implementation core. The SAGE anchor is built as a mixture: π_anchor = (1−λ) π_base + λ π_shape, where π_shape is a small set of mode templates — reasoning patterns the practitioner wants to upweight (e.g. “verify before answering,” “decompose into named subgoals,” “use auxiliary calculation,” “explicit abstention”). The shape distribution is itself a small LM fine-tuned on a curated mode catalog, not hand-engineered token-level priors. The mixing coefficient λ is annealed: high early in training (strong pull), low late (let RLVR do the rest). The whole pipeline is orthogonal to GRPO/POPO/etc. — it modifies only the KL anchor; the optimizer, the reward, and the sampling scheme are unchanged. (3) The empirical results. Section 5 reports pass@1 and pass@k on math, code, and logic benchmarks. The headline: SAGE is the only exploration intervention in their comparison table that improves pass@k without degrading pass@1 (most exploration methods buy diversity at the cost of accuracy; SAGE buys both). The pass@k gap is largest on problems where the base model assigns negligible probability to the correct reasoning skeleton — exactly the regime where standard RLVR fails to discover. They also report a clean ablation: replacing π_shape with a uniform-over-tokens distribution recovers the standard entropy-bonus result; replacing it with a sharpened version of π_base recovers vanilla RLVR; the mode-catalog version is qualitatively different. For your purposes, three takeaways. (a) SAGE composes with Boltzmann Projection. If you set the policy’s long-run target to the binary-reward Boltzmann distribution (per the May 20 deep dive) and shape the anchor with SAGE, you get a two-sided statement: the optimal-distribution side from the projection paper, the gradient-direction side from SAGE. That’s the cleanest formal articulation of “policy selection via support-shaped reference” we’ve had. Worth writing up as a unified note while it’s fresh. (b) SAGE ports to formal-methods RL with minimal modification. Replace the mode catalog with a curated set of proof skeletons — induction-on-n, case-split-on-cases, contradiction, generalize-then-specialize, rewrite-chain. The shape distribution becomes a small Lean-specific LM trained on Mathlib proof skeletons grouped by structural template. The KL anchor then explicitly pulls the prover toward attempting structurally diverse proofs, not just textually different ones. This is the missing piece between FormalRewardBench’s reward-side framing and the policy-side machinery you’ve been collecting. (c) The mode-catalog → threat-taxonomy bridge. Connect with Thursday: the Controllable PDS error taxonomy and the SAGE mode catalog are dual structures — one enumerates ways to be wrong (for PRM training), the other enumerates ways to be diversely right (for anchor shaping). The PRM scores trajectories against the taxonomy; the anchor pulls toward the mode catalog; together they form a coordinate system on the space of reasoning chains. The LIFR introduction has its third paragraph now: policy side · data side · reference side, with the joint geometry being the substantive claim.

Process-Verified Reinforcement Learning for Theorem Proving via Lean
OpenReview · May 2026·~4 min read
The Lean-side companion to Thursday’s Controllable PDS deep dive, by a different group with a very different solution to the same problem. The setup: treat the Lean elaborator as a symbolic process oracle — not just “does the proof typecheck?” (outcome) but “which is the earliest tactic that fails to elaborate, and which prior tactics are locally sound?” (process). Two mechanisms worth tracking. First-error propagation: once Lean’s elaborator flags tactic t_k as the first failing step, all subsequent tactics inherit a structured negative signal; tactics before t_k inherit a positive sound-step signal, not a discounted version of the final binary outcome. First-token credit: within a tactic, the credit lands on the token that committed to the failed move (the lemma name, the rewrite target), not spread uniformly across the tactic. Both are fused into a GRPO-style objective that interpolates outcome and process advantages. The contrast with PDS is the right way to think about this: PDS synthesizes step-labeled training data offline using a corruption-then-recompute pipeline; Process-Verified RL extracts step labels online from the elaborator’s own structural feedback. They’re complementary — you could in principle train a PRM on PDS-style synthesized data and then RL with Process-Verified credit using the same PRM as a second-stage scorer. For your work, this paper is closer to the “Direction-2 LIFR” sketch than anything else this month; it’s the engineering instantiation of the Lean-elaborator-as-symbolic-engine port you wrote in Thursday’s margin note.
Formal Methods RLVR Read Trending
Rethinking RL for LLM Reasoning: It’s Sparse Policy Selection, Not Capability Learning
arXiv · May 7, 2026·~4 min read
The empirical paper most aligned with the selection-principle frame you’ve been sharpening for the LIFR draft. Claim: RLVR’s reasoning gains aren’t evidence of new capability; they’re evidence of sparse policy selection at entropy-gated decision points. The method, ReasonMaxxer: identify the small set of tokens where the base model’s entropy is high (the “decision points”), apply a contrastive loss to push toward the eventually-correct continuation at those tokens, and stop. No reward model, no rollouts, no GRPO — just a targeted contrastive update at ~10–100 tokens per example. The reported result is striking: matches or exceeds full RLVR on math benchmarks using tens of problems and minutes of single-GPU training — roughly three orders of magnitude cheaper than full RL pipelines. The right way to read this is not “RLVR is over.” The right way: this is the strongest evidence yet for the selection-vs-capability decomposition you’ve been pushing. If ReasonMaxxer reproduces RLVR with three OOM less compute by operating only at high-entropy decision points, then RLVR’s capability footprint is just those decision points; the rest of the gradient is moving mass between equivalent outputs. This is the empirical foundation the LIFR “policy selection over a base” argument has been missing. Pair with SAGE: ReasonMaxxer says “only the entropy-gated tokens matter”; SAGE says “the anchor controls which entropy-gated tokens get explored.” Same underlying view from two angles.

Exploration-Driven Optimization (EDO) for Test-Time LLM Reasoning
arXiv · May 14, 2026 · Promoted·~4 min read
Test-time reasoning enhancement framed as an exploration-exploitation balance over a search tree of candidate continuations, with the explore/exploit decision parameterized by a learned per-step uncertainty estimator. EDO doesn’t train the policy at all; it modifies the inference-time search to spend more samples on under-explored prefixes when the uncertainty model says the current best is brittle. The link to today’s deep dive: SAGE shapes where the policy gets pulled during training; EDO shapes where the same policy spends compute at inference. If you adopt both, the practical recipe is “train with a mode-aware anchor, then deploy with a mode-aware search.” The interaction is non-obvious and worth a margin note: does a SAGE-trained policy need EDO-style search, or does the mode-shaped training make the inference distribution naturally well-spread? The paper’s ablations on policies of different training recipes don’t include anchor-shaped baselines, so the question is open — potentially a quick experiment in the LIFR appendix.
Promoted RLVR Read
Nathan Lambert — “How open model ecosystems compound”
Interconnects · May 12, 2026·~3 min read
The cost-structure essay that’s been quietly reframing how people think about why DeepSeek and Qwen keep pulling ahead on the open leaderboards. Lambert’s key decomposition: the compute to train the final model end-to-end is a small fraction of the compute to build the leading model. Most of the spend is R&D — failed runs, hyperparameter sweeps, data ablations, post-training recipes. In a closed-lab regime, every lab pays that R&D cost privately. In an open-ecosystem regime (Chinese labs, Western open-weights efforts), the R&D learnings get socialized across the ecosystem — the next lab’s post-training pipeline starts from the previous lab’s public recipe, not from scratch. The implication: open ecosystems have a lower marginal R&D cost per generation, which compounds across generations in a way that’s invisible if you only look at “cost to train the final checkpoint.” The framing matters for your reading because most of the RLVR papers you’ve been tracking come out of exactly this socialized-R&D regime — the post-training recipe is the moat, and the moat is leaking by design. Worth pairing with the May 4 “distillation panic” piece if you haven’t finished it.
