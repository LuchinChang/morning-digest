CktFormalizer: Autoformalization of Natural Language into Circuit Representations (Lean)
arXiv · May 2026·~22 min read
A direct attempt at the autoformalization pipeline you keep circling: take an English-language spec for a digital circuit, produce a Lean 4 proof obligation that captures it, and discharge the obligation against an RTL implementation. The pipeline has three stages worth dwelling on. (1) Spec extraction: a small LLM converts the natural-language description into a structured intermediate (interface signature, timing assumptions, safety/liveness properties). (2) Lean lift: each intermediate-form clause is mapped to a Lean theorem schema, with a fixed library of circuit primitives doing the typing work — this is the part you should read with paper, because it’s the place where the autoformalization either succeeds cleanly or accumulates technical debt. (3) Checker loop: the produced theorem is fed to a tactic-based prover, and failures are routed back to the LLM as structured feedback (counter-trace, missing invariant, type mismatch). The empirical headline is ∼71% end-to-end success on a curated benchmark of open-source hardware specs — up from ∼38% for the same LLM without the structured intermediate. Two things to connect to your own work. First, the structured intermediate is the formal-methods version of a chain-of-thought: it’s the place where the verifier can actually intervene during generation, unlike post-hoc spec checking. Compare this to LIFR’s contract-synthesis loop (your April 27 read) — CktFormalizer is doing the same trick at the level of a domain-specific spec language. Second, the checker loop is a natural RLVR setup that the paper does not exploit: every failure mode in stage 3 is a verifiable reward signal. The obvious follow-on is a GRPO-style fine-tune over the LLM in stage 1 with the Lean checker in stage 3 as the verifier — which is precisely the kind of paper that would sit in your wheelhouse. Sketch the diagram in §3 with the RL arrow you’d add. That’s the dissertation chapter.

GXPO: Gradient Extrapolation-Based Policy Optimization
arXiv · May 9, 2026·~4 min read (skim)
A plug-compatible GRPO variant. Idea: do a few extra backward passes during an “active phase” of training to approximate a longer local lookahead, then extrapolate the gradient direction. Cost: three backward passes instead of one in that phase. Result: +1.65 to +5.00 pass@1 over GRPO on Qwen2.5 and Llama math benchmarks. The reason this matters more than the headline number: GXPO is essentially second-order GRPO done cheaply, which is the move that has been missing from the post-GRPO design space. Read §3 (the extrapolation construction) and §5.2 (where they ablate the lookahead horizon — the curve is informative).
RLVR Trending Read
VHG: Verifier-Backed Hard Problem Generation for Mathematical Reasoning
arXiv · May 9, 2026·~4 min read (skim)
The setter-solver duality with a third party. Standard adversarial curriculum has a setter that proposes problems and a solver that solves them; VHG inserts an independent verifier that audits the setter’s problems for validity (well-posedness, ground-truth resolvability) before they hit the solver. The setter’s reward is jointly determined by problem validity and solver difficulty, which closes the obvious loophole where the setter ships unsolvable nonsense for free reward. Connect this to today’s deep dive: CktFormalizer is the inverse construction — a verifier that certifies a spec rather than an auditor that detects pathology — and the two together sketch a verifier-mediated curriculum pipeline for hardware-style RLVR.

Reward Hacking in the Era of Large Models: Mechanisms, Emergent Misalignment, Challenges
arXiv · April 2026·~4 min read (skim)
The survey-but-actually-a-framework paper. The thesis is unification: reward hacking, specification gaming, and reward over-optimization are all consequences of optimizing an expressive policy against a compressed reward representation, with the failure shape determined by the interaction of three forces — objective compression, optimization amplification, and evaluator-policy co-adaptation. The paper explicitly walks RLHF, RLAIF, and RLVR through the same lens, which is the part most relevant to you: the “LLMs Gaming Verifiers” paper you read April 29 falls out as a special case (the verifier is the compressed object). Skim §2 (the three-force model) and §5 (the taxonomy — useful as a checklist when reading new RLVR papers).
RLVR Formal Methods Trending Read
Demystifying GRPO: Its Policy Gradient is Secretly a U-Statistic
arXiv · March 2026·~3 min read (skim)
The cleanest theoretical reading of GRPO to date. Observation: the group-relative advantage is precisely a degree-2 U-statistic, which lets the authors borrow off-the-shelf U-statistic machinery to characterize GRPO’s finite-sample MSE, its asymptotic distribution, and the suboptimality gap of the resulting policy. Two payoffs. (1) GRPO is shown to be asymptotically equivalent to an oracle policy gradient with access to a true value function — i.e., the group-normalization is doing implicit critic-fitting, which is the same observation POISE made last week from a completely different angle. (2) A universal scaling law for the optimal group size falls out, which is concrete advice for anyone training GRPO. Pairs naturally with today’s GXPO read — GXPO is GRPO + lookahead, this paper is GRPO = U-statistic.
