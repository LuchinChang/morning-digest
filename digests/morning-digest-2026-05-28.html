“When solving a problem of interest, do not solve a more general problem as an intermediate step. Try to get the answer that you really need but not a more general one.”

— Vladimir Vapnik, Statistical Learning Theory (1998), the imperative of discriminative methods. Today’s arc: discriminate, don’t generate. Yesterday’s three positions all asked “which token carries the credit?” — action tokens (ActFocus), reasoning tokens (MaR), or simplex-direction (Listwise). Today DelTA opens a fourth position: the wrong question. The right one is which tokens are discriminative — i.e., which token-gradient directions actually separate high-reward from low-reward responses, vs. which ones are just high-frequency shared patterns (formatting, boilerplate) that dominate the centroid by sheer count. The action-vs-reasoning split is one cut of the data; the discriminative-vs-shared split may be the cleaner one.

🔗 A fourth position on credit flow. Yesterday’s three-way debate — action tokens (ActFocus), reasoning tokens (MaR), simplex direction (Listwise PO) — turns out to share an implicit assumption: that the right way to partition the gradient is by token role. DelTA breaks that. Its diagnosis: the policy-gradient update direction is a linear discriminator over token-gradient vectors, and the discriminator’s centroids are dominated by shared high-frequency patterns (formatting, boilerplate, common reasoning connectives). These tokens are neither “action” nor “reasoning” in the LIFR sense — they’re just frequent, and frequency dominates averaging. So ActFocus’s “action tokens get 3–8× the gradient” finding may largely be reporting that action tokens happen to coincide with the high-frequency formatting cluster (tool-call brackets, search-query delimiters), and the underlying signal is being washed out by averaging across token-frequency-matched non-discriminative noise. DelTA’s test for the LIFR draft: partition tokens by discriminability, not by role. If LIFR re-runs the ActFocus token-energy diagnostic with a discriminability axis instead of an action-vs-reasoning axis, the “Action Bottleneck” may turn into a “Discriminative Bottleneck” that cleanly subsumes both MaR’s and ActFocus’s claims.
🌟 Deep Dive · 25 min
DelTA: Discriminative Token Credit Assignment for Reinforcement Learning from Verifiable Rewards
arXiv · May 20, 2026 · Zhang, Wu, Lin·~25 min read
The cleanest reframing of the credit-assignment debate to land this month. Three things to take from it. (1) The diagnosis — the discriminator view. Section 2 is the heart of the paper. Treat the RLVR policy-gradient update at a given prompt as a vector in token-gradient space; the authors show that this update is exactly a linear discriminator in that space, with positive- and negative-side centroids formed by advantage-weighted averages of token-gradient vectors over high-reward and low-reward rollouts respectively. The direction the policy moves is the centroid difference. This is a clean, basis-free way to talk about “what the gradient is doing” that doesn’t depend on a token-class taxonomy. The reason this matters: every prior framing in the credit-assignment literature (ActFocus, MaR, Listwise, ReBel, OPPO) is implicitly a choice of basis — ActFocus picks action-vs-reasoning, MaR picks regulation-vs-knowledge, Listwise picks the response simplex. DelTA picks no basis; it works directly with the centroid geometry. (2) The pathology — shared-pattern dominance. Section 3 is the diagnostic that should make you sit up. The authors measure the cosine similarity between the positive and negative centroids across a range of RLVR training runs. Across math reasoning, code, and agentic tasks, the centroids are highly aligned — cosine 0.7–0.95 depending on task. That means the linear discriminator is operating mostly along a direction that’s common to both sides; only a thin perpendicular component is actually discriminative. The cause: high-frequency tokens (formatting, common connectives, boilerplate) dominate both centroids because there are simply more of them. The discriminative component — the tokens that actually distinguish a correct trajectory from an incorrect one — is buried under shared-pattern noise. This is exactly the failure mode that “uniform credit” in vanilla GRPO produces, but it’s diagnosed at the right level: not “reasoning tokens get the same credit as action tokens” but “high-frequency tokens dominate the centroid average regardless of their discriminative value.” (3) The fix — coefficient reweighting. Section 4 introduces the DelTA reweighting. The construction is principled: for each token-gradient vector, estimate a coefficient that amplifies the side-specific component (the component that points away from the shared-centroid direction) and downweights the shared component. The coefficient is computed via a self-normalized estimator on the rollout batch — no auxiliary model, no annotation, no extra forward pass. The resulting effective update direction has a much larger discriminative-to-shared ratio (the cosine between positive and negative centroids drops from ~0.85 to ~0.35 in their flagship experiment). On performance: DelTA improves over GRPO by 2.1–6.4 pp across MATH, GSM8K, HumanEval, and ALFWorld, with the largest gains exactly where the prior credit-assignment literature said gains were possible — long-horizon agentic tasks. Three notes. (i) This subsumes the strong reading of ActFocus. If ActFocus’s “action tokens get 3–8× the gradient” finding is largely measuring frequency dominance (action tokens like search brackets, tool-call openers, environment-state markers are extremely high-frequency in agentic traces), then DelTA’s reweighting should reduce the action-vs-reasoning gradient ratio while improving performance — and that’s exactly what their Section 5.3 ablation reports. ActFocus’s diagnosis is right at the token-class level but wrong at the causal level. (ii) This makes MaR more interesting, not less. MaR’s metacognitive regulation tokens are rare (they’re a specific subset of reasoning behavior, not generic CoT padding). If DelTA’s discriminability framing is right, then rare-but-discriminative tokens are precisely where the signal lives, and MaR’s gains come from boosting these naturally-low-frequency-but-high-discriminability tokens above the formatting noise floor. The MaR × DelTA cross-test you should add to the backlog: run MaR’s composite reward on top of DelTA’s reweighting and check whether the gains compose. If they do, MaR is boosting a token class that DelTA was already amplifying for discriminability; if they don’t, MaR is overlapping with DelTA’s effective update direction. Either result is informative. (iii) For the LIFR draft, this is the unifying framing you’ve been waiting for. The four corners of the tetrahedron (policy, data, reference, metacognition) are different ways of increasing the discriminative-to-shared ratio of the effective update direction. Write the “Where does the gradient live?” section around DelTA’s discriminator view as the master frame, with action-token / reasoning-token / simplex-direction as three coarse partition schemes that each capture some discriminative axis but get tangled with frequency confounds. Add to backlog: Token-discriminability vs. token-class plot — for ALFWorld, plot the per-token cosine similarity to the discriminative centroid difference (DelTA’s axis) on the y-axis and token class (action / reasoning / format) on the x-axis; check whether discriminability and class are correlated or orthogonal. If they’re orthogonal, the entire token-class debate of the past month was a basis-choice artefact, and LIFR’s right move is to anchor on DelTA.

Advancing Mathematics Research with AI-Driven Formal Proof Search
arXiv · May 21, 2026 · Tsoukalas et al.·~5 min read
The headline formal-methods paper of the week, and probably of the quarter. Tsoukalas et al. build an LLM agent that generates Lean 4 proofs and search-loops against the compiler; the agent autonomously resolved 9 of 353 open Erdős problems at a per-problem cost of a few hundred dollars, proved 44 of 492 open OEIS conjectures, resolved a 15-year-old open Hilbert-function question in algebraic geometry, and improved an open bound in convex optimization by discovering a novel algorithmic parameter schedule. The methodology is “agent × Lean × library retrieval” in the same shape as OProver and Leanabell-V2, but the scale of the open-problem evaluation is what’s new. Two things matter for you. (1) The Lean 4 verifier feedback loop is doing the work here, which is exactly the RLVR feedback shape you’ve been arguing the LIFR draft should anchor on. The agent doesn’t need a learned reward model — the compiler is the reward model — and the per-problem-cost number suggests the loop is efficient enough that the binary verifier signal is dense enough to do useful credit assignment over multi-hour proof attempts. That’s a falsifying datum for the “binary verifier rewards are too sparse” family of claims (including Binary Rewards / Fundamental Challenges from your reading list). (2) The math itself is consequential. Nine open Erdős problems is the kind of result that changes how mathematicians use these tools, and your dissertation is exactly the kind of work that lives downstream of this shift. Read the methodology carefully (Section 3) and the failure analysis (Section 6); if you want to write a credible Lean-RLVR thread in the dissertation, this paper is now the natural citation for “the RLVR + verifier loop is empirically useful for open mathematics.” Strong candidate to promote to deep dive tomorrow if you want to spend more time with it.
Formal Methods Trending Read
LambdaPO: A Lambda-Style Policy Optimization for Reasoning Language Models
arXiv · May 19, 2026·~4 min read
Re-conceptualises the advantage estimation from a scalar to a decomposed pairwise preference structure — in the spirit of LambdaRank from learning-to-rank, hence the name. The advantage between two rollouts in a group is computed pairwise (with a lambda weight that encodes how much you trust the comparison), and the policy update aggregates over all pairs rather than over a single scalar mean. Two reasons to read this next to DelTA. (1) It’s also operating at a level above token-class — pairwise advantage is a response-level object, not a token-class object, so LambdaPO and DelTA are operating on complementary parts of the GRPO update: LambdaPO restructures the advantage signal (what each rollout contributes), DelTA restructures the token-gradient reweighting (what each token within a rollout contributes). They’re compatible interventions. (2) The pairwise-preference framing is the natural bridge from RLVR back to RLHF — Lambert’s “next leg goes back to preferences” argument from yesterday becomes operational with LambdaPO as the mechanism. If the dissertation needs to argue that the RLVR → preference transition can preserve verifier-signal density, LambdaPO is the construction to point at.

AEM: Adaptive Entropy Modulation for Multi-Turn Agentic Reinforcement Learning
arXiv · May 1, 2026 · Promoted·~3 min read
Per-turn adaptive entropy controller for multi-turn agentic RL: the entropy target is modulated turn-by-turn based on the local reward signal and the current trajectory’s entropy budget, rather than held fixed across the whole trajectory. The construction is simple (a PID-like controller on per-turn entropy that targets a learned setpoint) and the gains are modest-but-consistent across ALFWorld, WebShop, and a synthetic multi-turn task. The reason this matters for today’s thread: DelTA tells you what the policy gradient does given a batch of rollouts; AEM tells you what rollouts you get at each turn. The discriminative-vs-shared ratio of the centroid difference depends on the rollout diversity, which depends on the per-turn exploration policy. If your rollouts are entropy-collapsed in middle turns (a known failure mode of multi-turn GRPO), DelTA’s discriminator centroids will both collapse onto whatever the policy is currently doing and the discriminative direction will vanish — even with perfect DelTA reweighting. AEM is the exploration-side condition for DelTA to have something to work with.
