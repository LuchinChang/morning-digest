Formal Methods × RL · 45-minute reading plan

“Divide each difficulty into as many parts as possible and as may be necessary to resolve it best.”

— René Descartes, Discourse on the Method (1637), Rule II. Today’s arc: credit assignment as a curriculum problem. Saturday closed on the reference side (SAGE’s anchor as design choice) and the policy side (ReasonMaxxer’s entropy-gated selection). Today pivots to the signal side: if outcome-only RLVR can’t learn from a 0/1 reward on hard problems because correct rollouts never occur, you don’t fix it by changing the policy or the anchor — you fix it by changing the problem. SCRL says: don’t train on the hard problem at all; train on a sequence of verifiable subproblems whose successful rollouts do occur, and let the credit propagate through the curriculum. Descartes’ second rule was about where to divide; SCRL is the algorithm that divides for you, using the reference chain as the cleaver.

🌟 Deep Dive · 25 min
From Reasoning Chains to Verifiable Subproblems: Curriculum RL Enables Credit Assignment for LLM Reasoning
arXiv · May 22, 2026·~25 min read
The cleanest data-side answer to RLVR’s credit-assignment problem this month, and the most direct fit yet with your LIFR “process taxonomy” sketch. Three pieces to read carefully. (1) The diagnosis. Sections 1–2 sharpen the standard complaint about outcome-only RLVR: on hard problems where pass@1 of the base model is ≈ 0, GRPO/PPO get essentially no learning signal because the entire group rolls out wrong. Standard fixes are policy-side (advantage reshaping, entropy bonuses, anchor shaping) or reward-side (PRMs, intermediate verifiers). SCRL’s move is orthogonal: change the problems, not the learner. (2) The construction. Section 3 is the implementation core. Given a hard problem P with a reference reasoning chain C = (c_1, …, c_n), SCRL parses C into a sequence of verifiable subproblems (P_1, …, P_k) of increasing difficulty, with the final P_k = P itself. Each P_i is constructed so that (a) its answer is a verifiable span (a number, an algebraic expression, a structured object) and (b) its solution chain is a contiguous prefix of C. Concretely, for math problems they split on equation chains and named-quantity introductions; for code problems on function boundaries; for logic problems on lemma-introduction points. The split is mechanical, not learned. Each subproblem comes with a structured response template so the rewarder can locate the answer span without having to parse the whole rollout. (3) The mixed group. Section 4 introduces the key training trick. A naive curriculum approach would train on P_1, then P_2, then P_k sequentially — this risks the standard catastrophic-forgetting problems. SCRL instead trains simultaneously on the entire ladder: a single GRPO group contains rollouts on P_1, P_2, …, P_k, and advantages are normalized within each subproblem (not across the whole group). The subproblem-level normalization is the load-bearing detail: it makes the easy subproblems contribute their reward signal even when the hard ones still pass@1 at zero, while preventing the easy ones from dominating the gradient by virtue of their higher success rate. Section 5’s ablations show that flattening normalization (i.e., one global advantage per group, treating subproblems and the original problem symmetrically) collapses to vanilla GRPO and gains nothing — the per-subproblem normalization is what makes the curriculum a curriculum rather than just data augmentation. The empirical headline: on MATH and a subset of CompetitionMath where standard GRPO has pass@1 below 5%, SCRL lifts pass@1 to 40–60% using only the reference chains that were already in the training set — no new annotation, no PRM, no verifier beyond the outcome checker that GRPO already uses. The win is largest on exactly the regime where outcome-only RLVR fails: hard problems with rare correct rollouts. Three notes for your LIFR draft. (a) SCRL is the data-side partner to SAGE and ReasonMaxxer. SAGE shapes π_ref to put mass on under-explored modes; ReasonMaxxer identifies the entropy-gated decision points; SCRL constructs the subproblem structure that lets the verifier give per-step credit. The three combine cleanly: shape the anchor × pick the decision-point × densify the reward, and you have the cleanest articulation of the “selection over a structured chain space” frame you’ve been building. (b) SCRL ports to formal-methods RL almost trivially. Lean proofs already are a sequence of verifiable subproblems — each tactic that successfully elaborates is, by construction, a verifiable subgoal. The Lean port of SCRL is: instead of parsing English reference chains into subproblems, take the reference tactic sequence (P_1, …, P_n), construct intermediate “prove this lemma” subproblems whose answer-spans are the lemma statements themselves, and train with per-subproblem-normalized GRPO. This is the dual of the Process-Verified RL paper from Saturday: PVRL extracts step labels online from the elaborator; SCRL-Lean would construct the step structure offline from the reference proof. They’re complementary: PVRL handles cases where the policy diverges from the reference; SCRL handles cases where the reference itself is the curriculum. Together they should compose into a full credit-assignment stack for Lean. (c) The cleanest counterexample to ReasonMaxxer. ReasonMaxxer’s strong claim is that RLVR is sparse policy selection at entropy-gated tokens — the rest of the gradient is noise. SCRL is the cleanest empirical test of that claim: if ReasonMaxxer is right, SCRL’s gains should disappear when you restrict updates to the entropy-gated tokens within each subproblem. The paper doesn’t run this ablation. It’s a single-GPU experiment, two weeks of work, and the result either way is a paper. Add to the LIFR-experiment backlog.

⚡ Quick Reads · 15 min total
CAM-Bench: A Benchmark for Computational and Applied Mathematics in Lean
arXiv · May 17, 2026·~4 min read
The first serious Lean theorem-proving benchmark that isn’t MiniF2F-style olympiad geometry: 1,000 Lean 4 proof targets in optimization, numerical linear algebra, and numerical analysis, adapted from undergraduate and graduate textbook exercises. The methodologically interesting piece is the dependency-recovery pipeline: textbook problems are notoriously context-dependent (“by Theorem 3.2, …”), so the authors built a pipeline that reconstructs the local Lean context — definitions, notation, prior lemmas — needed to state each problem as a standalone goal. That pipeline is the part you should think about for your own benchmark work: it’s the missing infrastructure between “here is a textbook” and “here is a Lean target the prover can actually attempt.” The benchmark composition itself is the second interesting half: previous Lean benchmarks are dominated by algebra and combinatorics; CAM-Bench’s coverage of optimization theorems (convexity, KKT conditions, duality) and numerical analysis (convergence rates, error bounds, conditioning) is exactly the territory where RLVR-style theorem provers should be tested if the goal is genuine applied-math utility. For your work, pair with FormalRewardBench (Day 39): CAM-Bench is the problem set, FRB is the reward-model evaluation; together they form the “what to prove” + “how to score the attempt” pair the Lean RLVR community has been missing.
Formal Methods Read Trending
OPPO: Bayesian Value Recursion for Token-Level Credit Assignment in LLM Reasoning
arXiv · May 21, 2026·~4 min read
The token-level companion to today’s subproblem-level SCRL. OPPO (Oracle-Prompted Policy Optimization) attacks the same diagnosis from a different angle: standard GRPO assigns a single trajectory-level advantage to every token, which dilutes the signal at the small set of pivotal reasoning tokens where the rollout actually branches toward success or failure. OPPO replaces that with a Bayesian value recursion: treat each token as updating a belief over “will this trajectory ultimately succeed?”, with oracle signals (from a verifier or a stronger model) used as natural Bayesian updates of that belief. The token-level advantage falls out as the posterior change in success probability, which is sharp at decision-points and small elsewhere — exactly the shape that the ReasonMaxxer paper argued was the right signal to be tracking. Read together with today’s deep dive: SCRL densifies the reward at the subproblem level (curriculum), OPPO densifies it at the token level (Bayesian belief). Both are credit-assignment fixes; both leave the policy and the anchor untouched. The combination is the obvious experiment — SCRL’s curriculum trained with OPPO’s within-subproblem token credit — and the paper’s framing is clean enough that the combination is almost a drop-in.

GRPO is Secretly a Process Reward Model (Sullivan et al.)
arXiv · Sep 2025 (revised Apr 2026) · Promoted·~4 min read
The result is sharper than the title suggests. Standard story: GRPO uses an outcome reward model (ORM) and assigns the binary outcome reward to every token in the rollout, scaled by the group-relative advantage. Sullivan et al.’s observation: because rollouts in a GRPO group share prefixes, the per-token advantage computed against the group baseline is mathematically equivalent (under mild assumptions on the group sampling distribution) to a Monte-Carlo PRM signal at every prefix-branching point — the “outcome” reward becomes a process reward at every token where the group diverges. The proof is short and worth working through: it’s essentially a counting argument over which rollouts in the group share which prefix tokens. The follow-on construction, λ-GRPO, is a single-hyperparameter modification that makes this implicit PRM explicit: weight the per-token advantage by how much the group’s prefix-branching structure says that token contributed to the outcome distribution. They report consistent reasoning-task gains and faster convergence. For SCRL specifically: SCRL’s per-subproblem normalization is doing something structurally similar to λ-GRPO — it’s exposing the prefix-structure of the reasoning chain as explicit reward signal. The two papers should be cited together in the LIFR draft; the joint point is “outcome-only RLVR contains a latent process reward that becomes visible as soon as you respect the prefix structure of the rollouts.”
Promoted RLVR Read
Sebastian Raschka — “Recent Developments in LLM Architectures: KV Sharing, mHC, and Compressed Attention”
Sebastian Raschka · May 16, 2026·~3 min read
Raschka’s clean roundup of the three architecture changes driving the “long-context got cheap” story in this year’s open-weight releases: cross-layer KV sharing (multiple attention layers share a single KV cache, cutting memory by 2–4× with small perplexity cost), mixture-of-Hidden-state-Carriers (mHC, the carrier-routing trick used in the GLM and Qwen open releases), and compressed-attention variants (low-rank or learned-pool projections of the attention matrix). For your reading the relevance is indirect but worth the three minutes: the cost of serving long-context reasoning is collapsing faster than the cost of training it, which means the RLVR-on-long-chains experiments you’re tracking will be increasingly cheap to deploy if they work, and the bottleneck moves to the credit-assignment problem you’ve been focused on rather than the inference-cost problem the field worried about a year ago. Pair mentally with today’s SCRL: the subproblem-curriculum recipe is exactly the kind of training-time intervention that becomes much more attractive when inference is cheap enough to run the curriculum repeatedly during evaluation.
