“An optimal policy has the property that whatever the initial state and initial decisions are, the remaining decisions must constitute an optimal policy with regard to the state resulting from the first decisions.”

— Richard Bellman, Dynamic Programming (1957), the Principle of Optimality. Today’s arc: where in the trajectory does the gradient actually live? Bellman’s principle is the right anchor: the credit must flow back along the trajectory, but the question of which sub-trajectory carries the signal is what every paper today is fighting over.

🔗 The three positions on credit flow. Yesterday MaR proposed that the load-bearing reward signal is metacognitive regulation — a property of reasoning tokens (the model monitoring its own progress). Today ActFocus proposes the opposite: the trained-signal gradient already concentrates on action tokens, and the right intervention is to downweight reasoning-token gradients. Meanwhile Listwise PO frames the whole question at a higher level — GRPO is a projection of a target distribution onto the LLM response simplex, so “which token carries credit” is the wrong question; the right one is “which direction in the simplex does the group-relative advantage point?” The LIFR draft has to pick a side. Three live hypotheses for today: (H1) MaR is right and ActFocus is measuring the symptom not the cause; (H2) ActFocus is right and MaR’s regulation gains come from action tokens that follow regulation phrases, not the regulation phrases themselves; (H3) Listwise is right and the action-vs-reasoning split is a token-level artefact of a simplex-level optimization. The deep dive is exactly the place to test these.
🌟 Deep Dive · 25 min
Resolving Action Bottleneck: Agentic Reinforcement Learning Informed by Token-Level Energy (ActFocus)
arXiv · May 14, 2026·~25 min read
The most provocative empirical paper of the week, because its diagnosis cuts directly against the implicit assumption behind MaR, ReBel, and most of the process-reward literature. Three things to work through. (1) The diagnosis — the “Action Bottleneck”. Section 2 sets up the analysis: under standard PPO and GRPO, treat the per-token training signal as an energy landscape and look at where the magnitude concentrates. The empirical finding (Fig. 2–4) is sharp — across ALFWorld, WebShop, Search-QA, and synthetic multi-turn tasks, the gradient magnitude on action tokens (tool calls, search queries, the discrete environment-facing moves) is roughly 3–8× larger than on the reasoning tokens that precede them. Uniform-credit policy gradient still backpropagates the same trajectory return to every token, but the per-step effective update is dominated by action tokens. The authors call this the Action Bottleneck: reasoning tokens are uniformly credited, but the dynamics make their gradients small. That’s exactly the opposite of the assumption MaR makes — MaR’s composite reward gives an additional process-shaped signal to the reasoning portion of the chain on the grounds that outcome-only credit is too weak there; ActFocus measures that the trajectory gradient is already not where the literature claims it is. (2) The construction — an embarrassingly simple test. Section 3 is the cleanest experiment in the paper. ActFocus is a one-line intervention: multiply the per-token gradient by a learned mask that downweights reasoning tokens and concentrates the update on action tokens. No new reward, no auxiliary model, no curriculum — just a token-class-conditional gradient scale. Across four environments this improves learning effectiveness and training stability. The deltas are modest (1.2–4.8 pp on average task success) but the direction is the headline: removing reasoning-token gradient does not hurt performance, and sometimes helps. If that result replicates, MaR’s implicit theory of change is wrong. (3) The implications — three readings, all uncomfortable for the LIFR draft. (a) The strong reading: reasoning-token gradients are noise. Action tokens are what determine environment outcomes, the trajectory return is the only signal that ground-truth-relates to outcomes, and concentrating gradient on action tokens is just “putting the signal where the truth lives.” In this reading, MaR’s gains are an artefact of the metacognitive scorer subtly biasing the policy toward action choices that follow regulation phrases (because the scorer rewards the regulation phrase, the policy outputs the regulation phrase, and the next action token gets credit). Process reward becomes a roundabout way to do action-token credit assignment. (b) The intermediate reading: the Action Bottleneck is real but ActFocus is overcorrecting. Reasoning tokens carry small-but-real gradient that aggregates over many tokens to a meaningful contribution. MaR’s regulation signal amplifies the per-reasoning-token gradient enough to clear the action-token noise floor. Both effects exist; the question is the gradient ratio at which the strong reading breaks down. (c) The weak reading: ActFocus is an environment-specific phenomenon. ALFWorld and WebShop are heavily action-discrete; pure math reasoning (where MaR was tested) has no “action tokens” in the same sense, so the diagnosis simply doesn’t apply. The four-environment evaluation in Section 6 doesn’t include math benchmarks, which is the obvious gap. Three notes. (i) ActFocus does not refute MaR — they evaluate on different task families. But ActFocus does refute the generic argument that reasoning-token gradient is weak and needs process reward to lift it; on the agentic side, reasoning-token gradient is already a small minority of the total signal, and reducing it further does not hurt. (ii) The token-level energy framing is the right analytic tool here. It cleanly separates “what gradient does the policy see” from “what gradient does the literature assume the policy sees”. Borrow this for the LIFR draft — specifically, a token-class-conditional gradient-magnitude plot is the diagnostic you should produce for any new process-reward proposal before claiming it works because of process signal. (iii) The cross-paper experiment writes itself: run ActFocus and MaR head-to-head on a shared agentic benchmark with reasoning-heavy traces (SWE-Bench, the kind ReBel uses), and see whether ActFocus’s reasoning-downweighting cancels MaR’s reasoning-upweighting. If they cancel cleanly, the regulation signal is real but its gradient lives mostly on the action tokens that come after it. If they don’t cancel — if MaR + ActFocus beats either alone — then both reasoning- and action-token gradients carry distinct signal and the LIFR draft needs to model the two channels separately. Add to backlog: ActFocus × MaR on agentic-math (e.g., MATH solved by tool-using agent), measure token-class-conditional gradient before and after each intervention. This is the highest-leverage cross-paper experiment on the backlog now.

Self-Distilled Agentic Reinforcement Learning (SDAR)
arXiv · May 14, 2026·~4 min read
Same submission day as ActFocus, same agentic-RL diagnosis (trajectory-level reward is too coarse for long-horizon agents), opposite solution. Rather than re-shaping the gradient at the token level, SDAR adds an auxiliary on-policy self-distillation (OPSD) objective: a teacher branch sees privileged context, and its token-level signals are mapped through a sigmoid gate to either strengthen distillation on teacher-endorsed tokens or soft-attenuate negative teacher rejections. The big numbers are real: +9.4% on ALFWorld, +7.0% on Search-QA, +10.2% on WebShop-Acc over GRPO across the Qwen2.5 and Qwen3 families. The interesting wrinkle for today’s thread: SDAR’s teacher signal also concentrates on tokens, but it doesn’t share ActFocus’s diagnosis about which tokens dominate — the sigmoid gate is selectivity by positive-vs-negative gap, not by action-vs-reasoning. Read together with ActFocus, SDAR is what you get if you trust the dense token-level signal but don’t commit to a story about which token class it should live on. The two papers are the “structured” (ActFocus, class-conditional) and “learned” (SDAR, gap-conditional) variants of the same intuition: GRPO’s uniform credit assignment is leaving signal on the table.

Listwise Policy Optimization: Group-based RLVR as Target-Projection on the LLM Response Simplex
arXiv · May 9, 2026 · Promoted·~4 min read
The third position in today’s three-way fight, and the one that operates at the highest level of abstraction. Listwise PO recasts GRPO as a target-projection: the rollout group defines an empirical target distribution over the response simplex (the K-simplex of possible responses for a given prompt), and the group-relative advantage is the direction in that simplex from the current policy to the target. Two things matter for the LIFR draft. (1) The reframing dissolves the action-vs-reasoning question. If the policy gradient is a projection onto the simplex, the token-class-conditional energy ActFocus measures is just the gradient’s coordinate representation in token-space — not its underlying structure. The “Action Bottleneck” becomes an artefact of the basis you chose to look in. Two papers can both be right: ActFocus is right about the token-coordinate concentration; Listwise is right that the underlying update direction is basis-independent. (2) The reframing makes group size a first-class object. The paper’s headline result is a universal scaling law for group size: the optimal K depends on the geometry of the target simplex (specifically, on the entropy of the optimal response distribution), and you can derive a closed-form prediction from a small-K calibration run. That’s the kind of result GRPO empiricism has been chasing for a year — principled group-size selection from the data, not from sweep. Pair this with the U-statistic paper (Day 41 if you remember — Demystifying GRPO) and you have two complementary geometric framings of group-based RLVR: U-statistic on the sampling side, target-projection on the update side. Together they argue that the whole “which token carries credit” debate is at the wrong level. The LIFR draft should at minimum cite this in the “Discussion” section as the framing that lets the four corners of the tetrahedron (policy, data, reference, metacognition) be reasoned about as different projections of the same simplex-level optimization.
Promoted RLVR Read
Nathan Lambert — “What comes next with reinforcement learning”
Interconnects · recent·~3 min skim
Lambert’s argument: the next leg of RL progress is not pushing further into sparser, harder verifiable domains. Current RL training is helping models get more robust at the individual tasks that compose a long trajectory, and the long-horizon behavior is then assembled by prompting and giving the model more inference compute — not by doing sparse credit assignment over hour-long traces. The path he sketches goes back from verifiable rewards to human preferences (RLHF-redux), with verifiable rewards as the middle leg of a longer arc. For today’s thread this is the “maybe credit assignment is over-rated” voice in the room: if Lambert is right, the action-vs-reasoning-vs-simplex debate is a debate about the local structure of the easy regime, and the hard regime will need a totally different signal. Worth keeping next to today’s deep dive as the methodological reminder.
