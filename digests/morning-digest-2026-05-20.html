Reference-Sampled Boltzmann Projection for KL-Regularized RLVR: Target-Matched Weighted SFT, Finite One-Shot Gaps, and Policy Mirror Descent
arXiv · May 5, 2026·~25 min read
This is the paper that puts the diversity-collapse selection-principle triangle in a single frame. Where UCPO picks the uniform element of the correct set C(x) and GCPO picks the max-determinantal-volume element in semantic-embedding space, Boltzmann Projection picks the element obtained by exponentially tilting the reference policy by verifier reward — i.e. π*(y|x) ∝ π_ref(y|x) · exp(β·r(x,y)). Three pieces are worth slow reading. (1) The Boltzmann target as the unique fixed-reference KL-regularized RLVR optimizer. Section 2 shows that the standard KL-anchored RL objective max_π E_π[r] - (1/β)·KL(π||π_ref) has a closed-form solution — the exponentially-tilted reference, normalized to a probability distribution. This is the same construction as the Boltzmann distribution in statistical mechanics: the inverse-temperature β trades off “follow the energy landscape” (large β) against “stay close to the prior” (small β). In the β→∞ limit, the construction collapses onto the support of argmax r, which (for binary verifiable rewards) is exactly C(x) — and reduces to UCPO when the reference policy is uniform on C(x), to the base-tilted-on-C(x) policy when the reference is the base model. (2) The Target-Matched Weighted SFT identity. Section 3 is the surprise: a static, off-policy weighted-SFT objective — with importance weights w(x,y) = exp(β·r(x,y)) / Z(x), normalized per prompt — produces the same fixed-reference Boltzmann target under MLE. This is mechanically a one-step policy improvement: sample from π_ref, weight by exp(β·r)/Z, do MLE, get the Boltzmann optimum exactly. The catch is the finite one-shot gap: because your samples are from π_ref rather than π*, the importance weights have variance that grows in β and in the support mismatch between π_ref and π* — quantified in Theorem 4.2 as a finite-sample slack term that closes only as you refresh the reference. (3) Refreshed BOLT as KL Policy Mirror Descent. The conceptual punchline. If you iterate one-shot Boltzmann projection with a periodic reference refresh — π_k+1 ∝ π_k · exp(β·r), with π_ref ← π_k every K steps — the algorithm is KL-divergence Policy Mirror Descent on the reward functional, with the “mirror map” being the soft-Bregman-induced one. This connects the Boltzmann-tilt construction to the standard convex-analysis machinery from Beck-Teboulle. For your purposes, three takeaways. (a) The UCPO & Boltzmann Projection & Binary-Rewards papers are all working in the same probability geometry — tilted distributions on the correct set — differing only in which prior they tilt and whether they regularize to a finite or infinite β. The right unified write-up of this week’s arc is “three Boltzmann temperatures on C(x).” (b) For formal-methods: the natural prior π_ref for Lean proof search isn’t the base LM — it’s the tactic-typed policy, the one that respects Lean’s type discipline. Boltzmann Projection with π_ref = tactic-typed gives you a KL-anchor to well-typed proofs, which is the closest analog of “syntactic prior” in the formal setting. Sketch this for the LIFR draft. (c) The Policy Mirror Descent framing is what lets you import convergence-rate results: the BMD analysis in Hu-Ji-Telgarsky and the mirror-descent line in Lan’s book both apply, and they say something the RLVR literature hasn’t internalized — convergence to the Boltzmann target is sublinear in β for fixed reference, geometric with refresh. That’s the convergence-rate paragraph for the LIFR introduction you were stuck on.

FormalRewardBench: A Benchmark for Formal Theorem Proving Reward Models
arXiv · May 11, 2026·~4 min read
The first benchmark that asks “how well do current reward models score partial progress in Lean 4 proof search?” and the answer is — not well. The paper formalizes sparse credit assignment in formal-methods RLVR (long proofs where only the final QED line gets reward, even though the intermediate tactic sequence carries enormous useful structure) and shows that off-the-shelf reward models trained on natural-language reasoning transfer poorly to Lean proof traces. For your work this is the empirical gap between “UCPO on the correct set” and “UCPO on the tactic-sequence-up-to-equivalence set” quantified: the benchmark gives you something to evaluate against if you build the proof-equivalence-aware reward model you sketched in the Direction-2 note. The construction worth noting: they include both whole-proof reward-modeling tasks and stepwise tactic-scoring tasks, and they explicitly call out that current PRM-style training data is essentially absent for Lean. That’s a paper-shaped gap.
Formal Methods RLVR Read Trending
OProver: A Unified Framework for Agentic Formal Theorem Proving
arXiv · May 17, 2026·~4 min read
The agentic-loop sibling of the Lean in-context proof learning paper you read yesterday. OProver iterates: attempt proof, run Lean compiler, on failure retrieve compiler-verified proofs that look structurally similar, revise the attempt with the retrieved exemplars in context, repeat. The architectural lift over “naive in-context retrieval” is two-fold: (i) the retrieval index is built from compiler-verified proofs only — a strong correctness filter at retrieval time, much cleaner than “retrieve any plausible proof” — and (ii) the revision step uses Lean’s error trace, not just the failure boolean, to focus the next attempt on the specific subgoal that failed. Read this as the agentic manifestation of the Boltzmann Projection idea: each iteration is one-shot tilt + reference refresh, where the “reference” is the population of verified proofs and the “reward” is “compiles in Lean.” The framing in the paper isn’t mathematical — it’s an engineering paper — but the algorithmic shape is exactly the refreshed-BOLT one. Worth holding both views in your head while reading.
Formal Methods Agents Read
Entropy Polarity in Reinforcement Fine-Tuning: Direction, Asymmetry, and Control
arXiv · May 13, 2026·~4 min read
The diagnostic the diversity-collapse literature has been missing. The paper defines entropy polarity: a signed, token-level quantity that predicts whether a given RL update will expand or contract the policy’s entropy. The core observation is sharp: reinforcing high-probability tokens contracts entropy (collapse); reinforcing low-probability tokens expands it (exploration). This is the gradient-level mechanism behind the UCPO / GCPO / Boltzmann Projection arc — they all change the objective so that the gradient stops disproportionately reinforcing high-probability tokens within the correct set. The asymmetry is the part to internalize: positive advantages on high-probability tokens cause >>collapse than equal-magnitude negative advantages cause expansion, so vanilla GRPO has a built-in entropy-contraction bias that’s structural rather than tunable. For your work, this is the diagnostic to add to the training loop: log polarity-decomposed gradient norm by token, plot it over training, and you’ll see diversity collapse coming before your Pass@K drops.
RLVR Trending Read
Not All Tokens Learn Alike: Attention Entropy Reveals Heterogeneous Signals in RL Reasoning
arXiv · May 10, 2026·~3 min read
Companion piece to Entropy Polarity, from a different angle. Rather than looking at policy entropy, this paper studies attention entropy — how concentrated the attention distribution is at each token — and shows it’s a strong predictor of which tokens carry the meaningful learning signal in RL reasoning training. Three tokens with the same loss can have wildly different attention-entropy signatures, and only the high-attention-entropy ones (broad attention, integrative reasoning) actually drive improvement. Pair this with EP-GRPO from yesterday: EP-GRPO used policy-entropy change as an implicit process reward; this paper uses attention-entropy level as a credit-assignment heuristic. Both are saying the same thing in different vocabularies — the per-token GRPO signal is hugely heterogeneous, and uniform credit assignment leaves a lot on the table. Useful to scan as a 3-minute piece; the table comparing attention-entropy vs. token-loss across reasoning benchmarks is the figure.
