🌟 Deep Dive · 25 min
FEST: Boosting RLVR via Randomly Selected Few-Shot Guidance
arXiv · May 2026 · Promoted from bookmarks (Day 28 carry)·~25 min read
Queued since the dense-signal question went live, read today: FEST is the cheap-source endpoint of the credit-propagation row, and the slot exists to read it directly against yesterday’s dGRPO. dGRPO manufactures its dense signal by training a teacher and distilling it on-policy; FEST manufactures a dense signal by dropping a handful of randomly selected few-shot demonstrations into the rollout context and letting them reshape where the policy explores — no teacher trained, no reward model, no extra forward pass at update time. The bet is Vapnik’s imperative made operational: if the only thing the sparse reward is missing is a denser gradient near good trajectories, a few worked examples may supply it without solving the harder problem of learning a teacher first. Three things to extract. (1) Where the demonstrations act. Are the few-shot examples purely a context prefix that biases the rollouts (so the “dense signal” is really an exploration prior), or do they also enter the advantage/reward computation? This is the load-bearing distinction for the grid — if it’s context-only, FEST belongs in the exploration row, not credit propagation, and the whole framing shifts. (2) Why “randomly selected.” The title’s provocation is that random demonstrations work; read the ablation that pits random against curated/retrieved selection. If random ties curated, the gain is regularization of exploration, not information transfer — and that’s a sharper claim than the paper’s headline, because it says the demonstrations matter as noise structure, not as content. (3) The cost-matched comparison. Hunt for any head-to-head against a distilled-teacher or process-reward baseline at equal compute. dGRPO’s whole justification is that the teacher term earns its training cost; FEST is the null hypothesis that says it might not. Action: add a cost column to the credit-propagation entry — teacher-distilled (dGRPO), demonstration-injected (FEST), turn-structured (TL-GRPO), verifier-supplied (Leanabell) — and rank them by signal-quality-per-unit-compute, flagging which comparisons the literature actually runs versus which are still assumed. ⚡ Quick Reads · 15 min total
TL-GRPO: Turn-Level RL for Reasoning-Guided Iterative Optimization
arXiv · Jan 2026 · surfaced this week·~5 min read
The structural counterpoint to today’s deep dive, and a direct descendant of Tuesday’s HiPER. Where FEST enriches the signal with demonstrations, TL-GRPO leaves the signal alone and shrinks the unit of credit: vanilla GRPO’s coarse, trajectory-level advantage gives every turn in a multi-turn rollout the same outcome reward, so a high-quality turn and the low-quality turn that followed it get updated identically — noisy gradients, unstable training. TL-GRPO assigns advantage per turn instead. Read it as the third family of answer to long-horizon credit: enrich the signal (dGRPO, FEST), restructure the agent (HiPER), or refine the granularity at which the existing signal is applied (TL-GRPO). The grid question: are finer granularity and a denser signal substitutes, or does turn-level credit make the demonstration injection sharper because each turn now has its own target? Skim for how turns are delimited and whether the per-turn advantage is estimated or assigned by rule.
RLVR Trending Read
Improving Lean4 Autoformalization via Cycle Consistency Fine-tuning
arXiv · Mar 2026 · formal-methods anchor·~4 min read
The RL-plus-formal-methods anchor, and the sharpest contrast in today’s set. Yesterday’s Leanabell-Prover-V2 had it easy: the Lean 4 verifier supplies a free, noise-free per-step signal because proving is machine-checkable. Autoformalization isn’t — the verifier can tell you the Lean compiles, but not that it means the same thing as the English. So this paper trains with GRPO against a learned cycle-consistency reward: translate NL→Lean4→NL′ and score the cosine similarity of the round trip. That makes it the formal-methods instance of exactly today’s theme — when the verifier goes silent on the dimension you care about (meaning, not syntax), you’re back to manufacturing a dense signal, and the question is whether an embedding-similarity reward is the FEST-grade cheap proxy or a reward-hacking surface waiting to happen. Read it against Leanabell as the two ends of the formal dense-signal spectrum: verifier-supplied vs. learned-proxy. Grab whatever they report on reward gaming — whether the cycle reward can be satisfied by a translation that round-trips but is formally wrong.
Formal Methods RLVR Read
FIPO: Eliciting Deep Reasoning with Future-KL Influenced Policy Optimization
arXiv · Mar 2026·~3 min read
The fourth source of dense signal, and the only one that needs nothing external. dGRPO borrows from a teacher, FEST from demonstrations, Lean4 from a learned proxy — FIPO shapes the gradient from the policy’s own future KL divergence, rewarding steps that keep later reasoning from collapsing toward a low-entropy answer too early. Read it as the “intrinsic” corner of the cheapness axis: if a self-derived signal recovers part of dGRPO’s gain, the teacher term’s justification narrows further. The grid question is whether future-KL shaping and demonstration injection touch the same failure (premature convergence) or different ones — skim the objective for how far “future” reaches and whether the KL term is a bonus or a constraint.
RLVR Trending Read
Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO), from scratch
Cameron R. Wolfe · Deep Learning Focus·~3 min skim
The Wednesday research-process slot, and a deliberate step backward. Four of this week’s papers are corrections to GRPO — denser teachers, finer turns, future-KL, demonstrations — and it’s worth five minutes to re-derive the thing they’re all correcting. Wolfe’s walkthrough is the cleanest statement of why GRPO drops the value function entirely and estimates the baseline from the spread of a group of rollouts, which is precisely the design choice every paper in today’s set is patching: a group-relative baseline is, by construction, a single scalar per prompt with no per-step structure. Read it to keep the critiques honest — each “dense signal” method is reintroducing exactly the per-step credit that GRPO threw away to avoid training a critic. The research-taste lesson is Vapnik’s again: know which simplification you’re paying to undo before you pay for it.
