🔬 Deep Dive · 25 min
Safe Reinforcement Learning via Recovery-based Shielding with Gaussian Process Dynamics Models
arXiv · Feb 2026 · AAMAS 2026 · GP dynamics · recovery policy · safety probability lower bound · ~25 min
The paper's contribution is a recovery-based shield that wraps a learned RL policy: at each step the shield consults a Gaussian-process model of the dynamics, asks "if I let this action through, what is the probability the system enters an unsafe set within k steps?" and, if that probability exceeds a threshold, swaps in a backup recovery policy trained to bring the system back to a known-safe region. The whole construction admits a provable lower bound on safety probability that's easy to read off in terms of the GP's calibration. The thing worth dwelling on for your proposal: this is the cleanest 2026 instance of a shield that doesn't require a hand-built model of the environment — the safety guarantee is parametric on the GP's epistemic uncertainty, which means the shield gets safer as data accumulates, not laxer. Read in conjunction with yesterday's Type-Checked Compliance, it gives you the two extreme points on the shield-design axis: "shield as a refinement type that won't elaborate" (Lean 4) vs "shield as a recovery controller backed by epistemic uncertainty" (this paper). Both can be cast as constraints on the RL policy's action distribution; what differs is which side of the feasibility check carries the proof obligation. Three concrete things to lift: (i) the calibration-conditional lower bound is a much better template for your proposal's "soundness" claim than the SMT-based bounds you'd been drafting; (ii) the recovery-policy idea generalizes to your shielded-codegen setting if you re-read "the unsafe set" as "configurations where the verifier discharges fewer obligations than before"; (iii) the GP becomes the explicit place where exploration cost shows up — useful for the section of your proposal that has to argue verification-aware exploration isn't strictly more expensive.

⚡ Quick Reads · 15 min total
On Tackling Complex Tasks with Reward Machines and Signal Temporal Logics
arXiv · Apr 15, 2026 · Reward Machines · STL · event-driven RL · ~4 min
Reward Machines have been around since 2018 — finite-state automata that encode the temporal structure of a reward signal — but the long-standing weak link is the event language they consume: usually hand-rolled propositional predicates over the state. The contribution here is to slot Signal Temporal Logic formulas in as the event-generator: each Reward Machine transition fires when an STL formula is satisfied on the trajectory so far. You get continuous, time-quantified events for free, and the Reward Machine stays small because it doesn't have to carry timing logic. For your proposal: this is the cleanest "the spec is the reward" instance you'll cite — the policy is rewarded exactly when the STL obligation is discharged, and the Reward Machine is the structural object that lets you compose obligations across phases. Pair with the deep-dive shielding paper: shield = "don't violate the STL formula"; reward = "discharge the STL formula." Same logical object, two roles.

FormalEvolve: Neuro-Symbolic Evolutionary Search for Diverse and Prover-Effective Autoformalization
arXiv · Mar 2026 · autoformalization · compilation gating · AST rewrites · ~4 min
Treats autoformalization as a compilation-gated, budgeted test-time search: the LLM proposes candidate formalizations, the compiler is the fitness function, and a small set of symbolic AST rewrite operators inject structural diversity that pure LLM mutation misses. Under a generator-call budget of T = 100, FormalEvolve hits 58% / 84.9% semantic-hit rates on CombiBench / ProofNet — strong relative to budget-matched baselines. The interesting structural idea: the AST mutators give you guaranteed syntactic diversity even when the LLM has collapsed onto a single template, which is the failure mode you've seen most often in your own Dafny experiments. File this with the LIFR paper from Sunday — both are pointing at the same shape of system (LLM proposer + symbolic critic + budgeted search), but FormalEvolve is the empirical artifact, LIFR is the research-vision write-up.
Formal Methods Read
AdverMCTS: Combating Pseudo-Correctness in Code Generation via Adversarial Monte Carlo Tree Search
arXiv · Apr 12, 2026 · MCTS · adversarial verifier · minimax codegen · ~4 min
The "pseudo-correctness" failure mode — code that passes the visible tests but harbors latent bugs the test suite never exercised — is reframed here as a minimax game between a Solver agent (proposing programs) and an Attacker agent (proposing inputs that should expose bugs). The two are interleaved inside a single MCTS tree: each Solver action expands into Attacker responses, and the verdict is the Attacker's best counter-test, not the visible test set. For your work this is interesting precisely because it makes the verifier active rather than reactive — the system spends compute generating the negative tests that would otherwise have to be authored by hand. Ties cleanly to SpecRL (which uses negative tests as a completeness signal for spec synthesis) and to the CIR/SR framing from the Apr 27 critique paper: the Attacker is essentially asking "is the candidate's reasoning causally grounded enough to survive an adversarial re-test?"
Agents Code RLVR Read
interwhen: A Generalizable Framework for Verifiable Reasoning with Test-time Monitors
arXiv · Feb 2026 · v2 (Mar 2026) · test-time monitor · single-trajectory steering · +15 pp · ~3 min
A single-trajectory verification framework: instead of sampling many rollouts and re-ranking with a verifier, interwhen runs one rollout and steers it on the fly using feedback from a verifier that checks intermediate verifiable properties. Up to +15 pp accuracy over standard chain-of-thought execution on the benchmarks they pick. The reason this matters for the formal-methods + RL bridge you're constructing: interwhen formalizes "the verifier is part of the policy, not part of the post-hoc filter," which is precisely the stance you keep arriving at from the other direction (start with a verifier and ask what the policy must look like). The "intermediate verifiable property" is exactly the obligation a partial proof or partially-elaborated Lean term can already discharge, so the construction lifts cleanly into your shielded-codegen setting.
