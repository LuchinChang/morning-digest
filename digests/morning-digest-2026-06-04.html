🌟 Deep Dive · 25 min
T2T: Boosting LLM Reasoning via Human-Inspired Reward Shaping (Thickening-to-Thinning)
arXiv · Feb 4, 2026 · Promoted from bookmarks·~25 min read
Off the bookmark shelf at last — five days late on Tuesday’s commitment, but here. The paper’s framing is unusual for the post-DeepSeek-R1 RLVR literature: rather than modifying the advantage estimator (DelTA, GCPO, EP-GRPO), the credit-assignment granularity (GRPO-VPS, segment-level), or the exploration controller (SAGE, AEM, Anchored PO), T2T modifies the reward schedule itself as a function of learning regime. The mechanism is two-phase. Phase 1, “thickening”: on incorrect attempts, the reward function actively incentivizes longer, more diverse reasoning chains — the model is rewarded not just for being closer to correct but for broadening the search space, generating novel solution paths, and exploring neighborhoods that would otherwise be pruned by the entropy collapse that GRPO induces. Phase 2, “thinning”: once the policy starts producing correct answers, the reward function flips to a length-penalty regime — the model is now rewarded for compressing the chain, removing redundancy, and converging on the minimal reasoning path. The transition between phases is per-problem rather than global: a problem the model has already mastered enters the thinning regime, while a problem the model is still struggling with stays in the thickening regime. The authors explicitly motivate the design from a Pólya-style observation about human mathematical learning — we explore broadly when stuck, consolidate aggressively when we have a path — and they argue (correctly, I think) that standard RLVR reward functions are monolithic in regime in a way that contradicts how human learning actually works. Three things to take from a careful reading. (1) The reward-schedule axis is the missing dimension in the LIFR draft. The two-taxonomy framing the draft committed to on Tuesday — credit-assignment geometry × exploration-control geometry — treats the reward shape as static and modifies only where and how aggressively the gradient pushes. T2T introduces a third orthogonal axis: when the reward changes shape as a function of the learning regime. On math benchmarks (MATH-500, AIME, AMC) the empirical gains over vanilla GRPO are non-trivial and the mechanism does not subsume any of the other axes — it composes with them. The LIFR draft’s unification grid should expand to credit-assignment × exploration-control × reward-schedule, a 4×3×2 cube rather than a 4×3 grid. The composition-experiment backlog doubles: in addition to Anchored-PO + GCPO (axes 1 & 2), we now need Anchored-PO + GCPO + T2T (all three axes) and a careful ablation showing that the three axes are genuinely independent. (2) The phase-transition heuristic is the operational hinge. The hardest empirical question in T2T is the same as in Anchored PO: when does the policy transition from thickening to thinning? The paper uses a per-problem rolling-accuracy heuristic (transition when the policy solves the problem correctly in ≥3 of the last 5 attempts), but this is brittle — it conflates “the policy has learned this problem” with “the policy got lucky three times.” The interesting transfer for Lean-RLVR is that the analogous transition signal in formal theorem proving is much cleaner: transition when the policy first proves the lemma without using sorry-elimination as a backup. That gives a noise-free phase-transition signal that the math-only setting cannot get. Add this to the experimental-protocol backlog as the Lean-RLVR contribution to the T2T mechanism. (3) The connection to the contrarian Sparse Policy Selection paper. Sparse Policy Selection’s framing — “RL is policy selection over the base model’s skill set, not capability learning” — predicts that T2T should fail to extend the reasoning boundary in any interesting way: thickening explores within the support, thinning consolidates within the support, and the support is fixed at the base-model boundary. The T2T paper’s gains on AIME/AMC are real but they are small enough that the Sparse Policy Selection critique is not refuted — T2T could be a regularizer that finds the highest-reward path within an existing skill set rather than a mechanism that pushes the skill set outward. The LIFR draft’s related-work section should now have a paragraph on each of the three axes (DelTA / Anchored PO / T2T) showing how each one fits or fails the Sparse Policy Selection frame. Action items: (i) commit the “reward-schedule” axis to the LIFR draft today and expand the unification grid to three dimensions; (ii) add the Anchored-PO + GCPO + T2T composition experiment to the protocol backlog as Figure 4; (iii) sketch the Lean-phase-transition heuristic (first non-sorry-elimination proof of the lemma) into the formal-methods application section; (iv) add the three-axis paragraph to the related-work section comparing each axis against the Sparse Policy Selection prediction.

PROGRS: LLM Reasoning with Process Rewards for Outcome-Guided Steps
arXiv · Apr 4, 2026·~4 min read
The natural extension of Tuesday’s GRPO-VPS thread. GRPO-VPS replaces the learned discriminator with an external symbolic verifier (Lean elaborator, type checker, constraint checker) and gets a provably sound credit signal at the cost of being restricted to formally-verifiable domains. PROGRS takes the opposite tack: it keeps the verifier learned — a frozen quantile-regression PRM paired with a multi-scale coherence evaluator — but fixes the well-known PRM pathology that locally fluent reasoning gets rewarded even when it ends in a wrong answer. The mechanism, “outcome-conditioned centering,” shifts PRM scores of incorrect trajectories to have zero mean within each prompt group, removing systematic bias while preserving informative step rankings. The centered process bonus drops directly into GRPO without auxiliary objectives or additional trainable components. Empirically it improves Pass@1 over outcome-only baselines across MATH-500 / AMC / AIME / MinervaMath / OlympiadBench, with stronger performance at fewer rollouts. The structural point for the LIFR draft’s “source-of-the-credit-signal” axis: PROGRS is a fifth point on the taxonomy. We now have learned single-pass discriminator (DelTA), prompt-contrastive discriminator (GCPO), self-referential entropy discriminator (EP-GRPO), external symbolic verifier (GRPO-VPS), and now outcome-centered learned PRM (PROGRS). The Lean transfer is direct — an outcome-centered Lean-PRM over tactic steps inherits the same bias-correction without giving up the soundness of the elaborator check at the final step. Composition note: PROGRS’s “no auxiliary objectives” design is exactly what makes it cleanly composable with both Anchored PO and T2T — one orthogonal axis each.
Formal Methods RLVR Trending Read
CAPO: Calibration-Aware Policy Optimization for Reasoning LLMs
arXiv · Apr 18, 2026·~4 min read
Another point on the discriminator-view axis — this time the “discriminator” is a calibration-aware AUC surrogate. The paper’s motivating observation is sharp: vanilla GRPO produces overconfident incorrect responses, where wrong answers yield lower perplexity than correct ones, and the relative calibration (AUC of correctness-vs-confidence) degrades monotonically with training. They prove the degradation is structural — GRPO’s uncertainty-agnostic advantage estimation provably misaligns the gradient with calibration — and propose CAPO, which uses a logistic AUC surrogate loss (theoretically consistent, admits regret bound) plus a noise-masking mechanism for stable dynamics. The empirical gains are credible: +15% calibration improvement and matching-or-better accuracy than GRPO, plus a Pareto-optimal precision-coverage trade-off when the model is allowed to abstain. The structural point for the LIFR draft: CAPO is the first paper in the discriminator-view taxonomy to explicitly target the calibration / accuracy joint objective rather than treating calibration as a downstream concern. The Lean-RLVR connection is interesting — in formal theorem proving, calibration matters because the agent’s confidence in a tactic gates whether to invoke a more expensive search subroutine. A CAPO-trained Lean agent should produce confidence scores that are actually usable as a search-budget controller. Add to the experimental-protocol backlog as a comparison point against the bare-GRPO baseline.

Read Trending
Open and Closed Models Are on Different Exponentials — Nathan Lambert
interconnects.ai · Jun 2026·~4 min read
Lambert’s macro frame for the open-vs-closed-model debate, which the dissertation’s “Why Now” section keeps inheriting whether or not the LIFR draft acknowledges it explicitly. His argument: the open and closed model ecosystems are no longer competing on the same axis. Closed models (Claude Opus 4.8, GPT-5, etc.) compete on margin per query — the economic question is whether enterprise users will keep paying for the marginal capability gap. Open models (Gemma 4, DeepSeek V4, Kimi K2.6, GLM-5.1) compete on capability per unit of self-hosting cost — the economic question is whether the cost of running them locally drops faster than the capability gap closes. Two different exponentials, two different equilibria. The structural point for the LIFR draft: the RLVR methodology arguments only matter for the closed-model side of the equilibrium — the open side is downstream of base-model capability and is mostly driven by the pretraining recipe rather than RL fine-tuning. The formal-methods chapter should be explicit that the Lean-RLVR contribution is targeted at the closed-model side: it improves per-query verifiability, which is exactly the axis closed models compete on. That framing also makes the dissertation’s claim about practical impact more honest — we are not arguing RLVR will displace pretraining; we are arguing RLVR is the dominant lever for the closed-model margin question.
