🌟 Deep Dive · 25 min
GCPO: Guidance Contrastive Token Credit Assignment for Discrete Policy Optimization
arXiv · May 28, 2026·~25 min read
Two-day-old paper, dropped while you were reading DelTA, and it is the cleanest mechanism you have seen yet for token-level credit assignment without an auxiliary reward model. The construction: at each rollout, the model generates token logits under two prompt conditions in parallel — a positive guidance prompt (the standard task prompt, possibly augmented with a hint that should make the task easier) and a negative guidance prompt (the same task with the guidance corrupted, neutralized, or replaced with an adversarial distractor). The per-token advantage is then proportional to the KL-divergence between the positive and negative conditional distributions at that token position. Tokens where the two distributions agree get near-zero advantage; tokens where they diverge sharply get large advantage. The interpretation is that large divergence = the token’s identity depends on the guidance, i.e., it is where the model is “using” the prompt to make a decision; small divergence = the token would have been generated regardless. GRPO’s sample-level advantage is replaced by this per-token contrastive signal, and the trajectory-level reward enters only as the sign and scale. Three things to take from a careful reading. (1) The connection to DelTA. DelTA reweights tokens by their position in a learned discriminator’s positive/negative centroid geometry; GCPO reweights tokens by their sensitivity to a prompt-level contrastive perturbation. They are isomorphic at the metaphor level — both ask “which tokens are discriminative?” — but GCPO’s answer requires no auxiliary network. That is methodologically cheaper and architecturally cleaner; the LIFR draft should present GCPO as the training-free instantiation of the discriminator view, with DelTA as the learned instantiation. The composition test: do they identify the same tokens as discriminative? If yes, the discriminator view has real explanatory power; if no, they are measuring different things and the LIFR draft needs a more careful taxonomy. (2) The positive-vs-negative prompt design. The authors are explicit that the choice of negative prompt matters: neutral “corruption” (random shuffling, blanking) gives one signal; adversarial corruption (replacing the guidance with a misleading hint) gives a stronger signal but is harder to construct. For formal-methods applications, the natural choice of negative prompt is swapping the goal statement to a known-similar but logically distinct goal — the agent’s tactic-block choice should be sensitive to which goal it is actually working on. This is a clean transfer of GCPO’s framework to a Lean-RLVR setting and you should sketch it into the LIFR draft today. (3) The empirical results on chain-of-thought. GCPO outperforms GRPO and DAPO baselines on math reasoning benchmarks by 2–4 percentage points, but the more interesting finding is the per-token visualization: the contrastive advantage concentrates on numerical-result tokens, logical-connector tokens, and the first token of each reasoning step — the exact tokens you would intuit are decision points. This is the most direct empirical evidence yet for the “token-level credit assignment matters because most tokens are filler” story. For the LIFR draft, this is your Figure 1: the GCPO heatmap on a math problem, contrasted with the uniform GRPO advantage, side-by-side. (4) The thread now. Three discriminator-view papers in 8 days (DelTA → MCPO → GCPO), operating at three different granularities (token-gradient → batch-rollout → prompt-contrastive). The LIFR draft can now be reframed as a unification paper: the discriminator view is the common abstraction, and the three mechanisms instantiate it at different levels of the optimization stack. That framing has a 10x better chance of being noticed than “another credit-assignment paper.” Action items: (i) write the “discriminator view as common abstraction” section of the LIFR draft today; (ii) sketch the negative-prompt design for a Lean setting (swap-goal-with-similar-distinct-goal) and add to backlog as an experiment to run; (iii) add to the Figure 1 backlog: GCPO heatmap on a math problem next to uniform GRPO advantage.

⚡ Quick Reads · 15 min total
Rethinking Supervision Granularity: Segment-Level Learning for LLM-Based Theorem Proving
arXiv · May 18, 2026·~4 min read
The bridge from yesterday’s Tsoukalas Lean/Erdős deep dive to today’s GCPO thread. The paper’s claim: the standard SFT/RL granularities for Lean — whole-proof or single-tactic — are both wrong. Whole-proof is too coarse (the model is rewarded for getting the answer right even when the intermediate tactics were lucky); single-tactic is too noisy (most tactic choices are forced by the local goal stack and carry no decision signal). Their proposal: segment-level supervision, where a “segment” is a maximal sequence of tactics that operate on a single sub-goal until a goal-stack change occurs. They show that segment-level training data extracted from Mathlib gives consistently lower loss and better generalization than tactic-level or proof-level on a Lean-Pile evaluation. The reason to read this next to GCPO and the Tsoukalas paper: all three are converging on the same insight from different sides. Tsoukalas’s tactic-budget-exhaustion failure mode says “the model often knows the next move but cannot execute it”; segment-level supervision says “the right unit to teach is the segment, not the tactic”; GCPO’s prompt-contrastive credit assignment says “most tokens are not decision points, only some are.” The synthesis the LIFR draft can claim: across formal proofs and reasoning chains, the unit of credit assignment should match the unit of decision-making — segments in Lean, contrastive-divergent tokens in CoT, batch-rollouts across domains in MCPO. The discriminator view is the common abstraction; the granularity is domain-specific.

EP-GRPO: Entropy-Progress Aligned Group Relative Policy Optimization with Implicit Process Guidance
arXiv · May 8, 2026 · Promoted·~4 min read
The third mechanism in the “dense feedback without an auxiliary reward model” trio (alongside DelTA and GCPO). The construction: EP-GRPO extracts a process-guidance signal from the model’s own internal entropy trajectory across reasoning steps — high-entropy steps that are followed by low-entropy continuations are interpreted as “productive decisions” (the model committed to a choice and the choice paid off in downstream confidence); high-entropy steps followed by high-entropy continuations are interpreted as “wasted decisions” (the commitment did not resolve uncertainty). The advantage signal is weighted by an entropy-progress alignment score. Empirically they report 1.5–2.5 pp gains on MATH and a sharper gain (~4 pp) on the harder olympiad subset. The interesting structural point for the LIFR draft: EP-GRPO operates on the model’s own information-flow geometry, GCPO operates on the prompt-conditional distribution geometry, DelTA operates on the gradient geometry of the policy update. Three different geometries, all yielding the same kind of dense per-token signal. The composition question: do all three identify the same “decision tokens”? If they converge, the discriminator view is robust; if they diverge, you have a real taxonomy to write about. For the LIFR experiments section: run all three on the same 100 reasoning trajectories and report the pairwise overlap of which tokens each method up-weights. That is a Figure-2-quality experiment.
Promoted RLVR Read
Rethinking RL for LLM Reasoning: It’s Sparse Policy Selection, Not Capability Learning
arXiv · May 7, 2026·~4 min read
The contrarian claim you need to engage with before committing the LIFR draft’s framing. The authors argue that RL’s observed gains on reasoning benchmarks are not capability learning at all — they are a sparse, predictable correction concentrated at high-entropy decision points where the model is uncertain which branch to take. The model’s base capabilities are entirely fixed by SFT; RL just routes the model toward branches it already could have taken. They demonstrate this with “ReasonMaxxer,” a minimal RL-free baseline that applies a contrastive loss only at entropy-gated decision points and matches or beats GRPO on several benchmarks. Why this matters for the LIFR draft: it is the strongest contrarian position to the “token-level credit assignment matters” story you have been building. If they are right, then the discriminator view is identifying the same decision points that any entropy-gated method would identify, and the “mechanism” (DelTA vs GCPO vs EP-GRPO) is incidental. The LIFR draft needs to address this directly: is the discriminator view doing something more than entropy-gated decision-point identification, or is it doing the same thing through a different lens? Two empirical tests would resolve this. (a) Compute the entropy of the model at each token position, and check whether the top-decile entropy tokens are the same as the top-decile GCPO-advantage tokens. If they overlap heavily, the contrarian claim is hard to refute. (b) Run a controlled comparison where entropy-gated supervision and GCPO are given the same compute budget; if GCPO wins, the discriminator view contributes information beyond entropy. Add both to the experimental-protocol backlog. Saturday-mood note: this is exactly the kind of paper a PhD student should actively look for — the one that threatens to falsify the framing you have been building. Engage with it now; do not bury it.
