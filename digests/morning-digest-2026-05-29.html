🌟 Deep Dive · 25 min
Advancing Mathematics Research with AI-Driven Formal Proof Search
arXiv · May 22, 2026 · Tsoukalas et al.·~25 min read
The deep dive promised in yesterday’s footer. Three things to take from a careful reading. (1) The methodology — agent × Lean 4 × library retrieval. Section 3 is the methodology to internalize. The agent is a Sonnet-class LLM in a loop with the Lean 4 compiler; on each step the agent proposes a tactic block, the compiler returns either “goals remaining” (with the new goal stack) or a structured error (with the type error’s span and the available local context). The agent decides next-step based on whether the goal stack shrank, whether new goals were created, and whether the error message localizes to a particular subterm. There’s no learned reward model in the loop — the compiler is the reward model — but the “reward signal” isn’t binary; it’s a structured object that the agent uses for local credit assignment. The library-retrieval module is a Mathlib-indexed RAG over lemma statements; the agent can request lemmas matching a goal pattern and the retrieval pre-filters by type compatibility. This is the closest live example of dense-feedback-via-verifier-channel rather than dense-feedback-via-reward-shaping. (2) The scale of the open-problem evaluation. Section 5 is what makes this paper consequential. 9 of 353 open Erdős problems resolved at a per-problem cost of a few hundred dollars; 44 of 492 open OEIS conjectures resolved; a 15-year-old open Hilbert-function question in algebraic geometry resolved; and an improved bound on an open problem in convex optimization via a novel algorithmic parameter schedule. The Erdős problems are not the easy ones — they’re problems that have been openly posted and unsolved for years, in some cases decades. The methodology section makes clear that the agent didn’t pattern-match these from training data: each resolved problem required a new construction or a new bound, formalized in Lean 4, that the human authors verified for novelty. This is the kind of result that changes how mathematicians use these tools, and the dissertation lives downstream of that shift. (3) The failure analysis — Section 6. The most useful section for the LIFR draft. The authors taxonomize 100 failed proof attempts and find three dominant failure modes: (a) goal-decomposition errors — the agent picks a too-aggressive intermediate lemma whose proof is harder than the original goal; (b) library-retrieval misses — the relevant lemma exists in Mathlib but the agent’s retrieval query is at the wrong level of abstraction; (c) tactic-budget exhaustion — the agent finds a proof sketch but the Lean elaborator times out on a particular sub-proof and the agent can’t recover. The interesting empirical claim is that (c) is the largest category by a meaningful margin — the agent often knows what to prove but can’t get the elaborator to accept it. That’s a different bottleneck than the “reasoning capacity” story the broader RLVR literature has been telling. For the LIFR draft, the relevant pull is: tactic-level credit assignment matters more than token-level credit assignment in formal proofs, because a single tactic block can encode an unbounded amount of computation that the Lean elaborator either accepts or rejects atomically. DelTA’s discriminator view operates at the token level, but the Lean failure mode is at the tactic level — one rung up. The composition question the LIFR draft should pose: does the discriminative-vs-shared geometry from DelTA generalize to discriminative-vs-shared tactics, where the “centroid” is computed over tactic-embedding vectors rather than token-gradient vectors? If it does, DelTA’s framing extends naturally to formal proofs and the dissertation has a clean cross-domain story. (4) Methodology note for your own work. Section 4.2 describes the agent’s prompt scaffold and the per-step temperature schedule (high temperature for tactic exploration, low for goal-decomposition); their reported per-problem cost includes both successful and failed runs in the denominator. The economics are real: if you build a Lean-RLVR loop with this shape, expect to spend ~$300–$800 per open-problem attempt at current API prices, and budget for a 5–20% solve rate on problems of comparable difficulty to the Erdős benchmark. For the dissertation, this is the empirical baseline to compare against. Action items: (i) re-read §3 with the LIFR draft open and note where “tactic-level” replaces “token-level” in their methodology relative to standard RLVR; (ii) star Section 6 in Zotero — the tactic-budget-exhaustion finding is the most quotable empirical anchor for the “density-of-feedback-channel matters more than density-of-reward” thesis; (iii) add to backlog: cross-domain replication of DelTA’s 0.7–0.95 centroid-similarity diagnostic, but with tactic-embedding vectors over a Lean training run; if the centroids are similarly aligned, the discriminator view ports cleanly to formal proofs.

⚡ Quick Reads · 15 min total
GXPO: Gradient Extrapolation-Based Policy Optimization
arXiv · May 9, 2026·~4 min read
Plug-compatible update rule for GRPO-style reasoning RL. The construction: rather than taking the standard policy-gradient step on each batch, GXPO performs three backward passes within an “active phase” and uses the gradient trajectory to extrapolate where a longer local lookahead would have landed. The cost is roughly 3× the per-step compute of vanilla GRPO; the benefit, in their reported numbers, is reaching comparable final performance in ~40% fewer outer-loop steps on MATH and GSM8K. Two reasons this matters today. (1) It’s a clean orthogonal axis to DelTA. DelTA reweights which tokens contribute to the update; GXPO reweights how far the update reaches by extrapolating along the local gradient field. Their compositions are independent — you should expect to be able to stack them — and if you do, the discriminator-view centroid geometry changes shape: GXPO’s extrapolated update direction is dominated more by the discriminative component (because the shared-pattern component tends to be locally flat) than the single-step direction is. Worth noting in the LIFR draft as the “temporal lookahead” complement to DelTA’s “per-token reweighting.” (2) The three-backward-pass cost is acceptable for a small lab. If you’re doing Lean-RLVR experiments at the scale the Tsoukalas paper suggests is possible (~$300–$800 per attempt), the GXPO multiplier is small enough to fit in budget — especially if it cuts outer-loop steps. Useful for the LIFR draft’s experimental-protocol section.
RLVR Read
Harmony in Diversity: Multi-domain Contrastive Policy Optimization for Large Reasoning Models (MCPO)
arXiv · May 25, 2026·~4 min read
Cross-domain GRPO variant. The idea: when you train a reasoning model on multiple domains (math, code, agentic), the structural relationships among rollouts across domains carry information that single-domain GRPO throws away. MCPO adds a contrastive term that promotes cross-domain knowledge sharing (rollouts that succeed in one domain pull representations that succeed in similar contexts in another) and in-domain consolidation (rollouts within a domain are encouraged to converge on a shared structure). The contrastive loss is computed over a learned embedding of rollouts; the authors report 1.5–3.2 pp improvements on multi-domain benchmarks. The reason to read this next to today’s thread: MCPO is operating at the response-batch level — it asks “which rollouts in the batch are discriminative across domains” — whereas DelTA operates at the token level within a single rollout. They’re complementary instantiations of the same discriminator-view metaphor at different scales. For the LIFR draft, MCPO is the natural citation for the cross-domain generalization claim: if the dissertation argues that RLVR’s gains compose across formal-methods and code-reasoning domains, MCPO is the construction that operationalizes that.

SU-01: Achieving Gold-Medal-Level Olympiad Reasoning via Simple and Unified Scaling
arXiv · May 13, 2026 · Promoted·~4 min read
The recipe: a 30B-active-3B MoE backbone, SFT on ~340K sub-8K-token reasoning trajectories curated for diversity and difficulty, then just 200 RL steps with a vanilla GRPO loss against a verifier. Reaches gold-medal-level Olympiad performance on AIME ’26 and a held-out IMO subset. Two things to take from this. (1) The 200-step number. If the recipe really does only need 200 RL steps on top of strong SFT to reach gold-medal-level reasoning, then the entire credit-assignment debate of the past month — ActFocus, MaR, Listwise, DelTA, GXPO, MCPO — is operating in a regime where RL is mostly a polish step, not the load-bearing training. The token-level credit-assignment story matters because the polish has to be efficient; but the heavy lifting is in the SFT corpus curation. (2) What this means for the LIFR draft. Two consistent framings. (i) The conservative framing: SFT does the bulk of the work; RL fine-tunes the discriminative-vs-shared ratio of the policy’s output distribution; DelTA / GXPO / MCPO matter because the 200 steps need to be efficient and the geometry of those 200 steps is exactly what those interventions improve. (ii) The aggressive framing: the “200 RL steps” claim doesn’t replicate at scale, and the actual contribution of RL is larger than the headline number suggests because the GRPO loss is being applied to a carefully-engineered curriculum that has been pre-filtered for RLVR-amenable signal. Read §4 carefully — their curriculum-construction protocol — before committing to either framing in the LIFR draft. (3) Action item for backlog. Re-run the headline SU-01 result with the GRPO loss replaced by DelTA reweighting and check whether the 200-step number drops further. If it does (say, to 100 steps), DelTA is doing what the discriminator-view story predicts; if it doesn’t, the SFT curriculum is already capturing most of the discriminative signal and the LIFR draft should foreground curriculum over update-rule.
