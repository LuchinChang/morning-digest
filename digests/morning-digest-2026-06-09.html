“An optimal policy has the property that whatever the initial state and initial decision are, the remaining decisions must constitute an optimal policy with regard to the state resulting from the first decision.”

— Richard Bellman (1957), Principle of Optimality. Tuesday framing: yesterday read the exploration axis; today the question moves one column over to how the learning signal propagates back. 🌟 Deep Dive · 25 min
dGRPO: Combining On-Policy Optimization and Distillation for Long-Context Reasoning in Large Language Models
arXiv · May 2026 · Promoted from bookmarks (Day 31 carry)·~25 min read
Queued Monday, read today: dGRPO is the long-context instance of the grid’s credit-propagation row, and the slot exists to read it through the lens the exploration axis just sharpened. The core mechanism augments vanilla GRPO — whose only signal is a sparse, outcome-level reward at the end of the chain — with a dense, token-level signal distilled on-policy from a stronger teacher. The claim is that in long-context settings, sparse reward alone degrades reasoning structure and induces forgetting of short-context skills, and that the teacher’s per-token guidance restores the gradient the sparse reward can’t supply. Three things to extract. (1) The composition of the objective. How are the two terms combined — a fixed mixing weight, an annealed schedule, a per-token gate? The credit-propagation axis needs to know whether the dense signal is a regularizer on the RL update or the dominant term with RL as garnish; the mixing rule is where that’s decided, and it’s the first thing the ablation should isolate. (2) The provenance of the gains. Read the ablations specifically for a teacher-only baseline (pure on-policy distillation, no reward) and a reward-only baseline (vanilla long-context GRPO). If dGRPO beats both, the interaction is real; if it only beats reward-only and ties teacher-only, then the RL objective is doing nothing and this is distillation wearing an RL hat — a distinction the grid must record, because it decides whether the row belongs under “credit assignment” at all. (3) The forgetting claim. The paper’s motivating failure is short-context skill loss under long-context RL; check whether the short-context retention is measured directly (a held-out short benchmark across training) or only asserted. That retention curve is the dissertation’s real interest — it’s the long-horizon analogue of the silent-vs-loud failure asymmetry, where the failure here is silent erosion of a capability the reward never names. Action: write the credit-propagation entry with the mixing rule named and the teacher-vs-reward decomposition logged; mark whether dGRPO’s gain survives the teacher-only ablation, because that single result decides if the row is a credit-assignment method or a distillation method mislabeled. dGRPO comes off the shelf (9 → 8), closing the Day-31 carry.

HiPER: Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning with Explicit Credit Assignment for Large Language Model Agents
arXiv · Feb 2026 · surfaced this week·~5 min read
The structural counterpoint to today’s deep dive. Where dGRPO fixes long-horizon credit by importing a denser signal from a teacher, HiPER fixes it by shortening the distance the signal has to travel — factoring the agent into a high-level planner and a low-level executor so each level’s credit is assigned over a horizon it can actually resolve. Read the two as the two families of answer to the same problem: enrich the signal vs. restructure the trajectory. The question for the grid is whether they’re substitutes or stackable — does a planner/executor split make the teacher’s dense signal redundant, or would dGRPO-style distillation still help inside each level? Skim for how the planner’s reward is defined and whether the hierarchy is learned or hand-specified.
Agents Trending Read
InftyThink+: Effective and Efficient Infinite-Horizon Reasoning via Reinforcement Learning
arXiv · Feb 2026·~4 min read
The long-context sparring partner. dGRPO and InftyThink+ both attack the degradation that shows up when reasoning chains get long, but from opposite ends: dGRPO keeps the chain and fixes the signal; InftyThink+ keeps the signal and fixes the horizon, segmenting unbounded reasoning into bounded windows that summarize forward so the effective context never blows up. Read it asking which problem is actually load-bearing for the dissertation — is long-context failure a credit-assignment problem (dGRPO’s premise) or a context-management problem (InftyThink+’s)? If both methods help and compose, that’s evidence the two failure modes are distinct axes rather than one mislabeled twice. Skim for the summarization step and any head-to-head against a plain long-context RL baseline.
RLVR Trending Read
Leanabell-Prover-V2: Verifier-Integrated Reasoning for Formal Theorem Proving via Reinforcement Learning
arXiv · 2026 · formal-methods anchor·~3 min read
The RL-plus-formal-methods anchor, and the cleanest contrast to the deep dive. dGRPO borrows its dense signal from a learned teacher; Leanabell-Prover-V2 gets its dense signal for free from the Lean 4 verifier — the prover proposes, the verifier checks, and the typed error is the reward, fed back to drive generation and correction. This is the formal setting’s structural advantage stated as an RL recipe: no teacher to distill, no reward model to train, because the environment itself supplies a noise-free per-step signal. Read it directly against dGRPO’s teacher term — the verifier is what a teacher is trying to approximate, and the gap between “machine-checked” and “distilled” is exactly the silent-vs-loud reliability axis. Grab whatever they report on verifier-feedback granularity: per-tactic, per-step, or end-of-proof.
