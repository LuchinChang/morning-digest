🔬 Deep Dive · 25 min
Learning to Reason with Insight for Informal Theorem Proving
arXiv · Apr 21, 2026 · PROMOTED from bookmarks (Apr 26) · DeepInsightTheorem · multi-stage SFT · technique-level pattern recognition · ~25 min
The framing claim of this paper — and the reason to give it a careful read rather than a skim — is that informal theorem proving has been treated empirically (does the model find a proof?) when it should be treated pedagogically (does the model recognize which technique applies?). Concretely, the authors curate DeepInsightTheorem, a dataset of ~14k informal-proof problems annotated with the canonical solution technique (induction variant, contradiction setup, generating-function trick, pigeonhole, telescoping, etc.), and design a three-stage SFT curriculum: (1) technique-classification pretraining, (2) technique-conditioned proof generation, (3) joint fine-tuning with both signals. The empirical result is roughly a 9-point bump on miniF2F-style benchmarks over technique-blind SFT baselines — modest but defensible. What's worth lifting for your proposal is not the technique vocabulary itself but the structural move: the authors decompose what was previously a single black-box objective ("emit a valid proof") into two composable signals ("recognize the technique" + "execute the technique"), and they show the decomposition is empirically helpful. Translate that to your domain. Shielded codegen currently treats "emit code that passes the shield" as a single black-box objective. The Insight-Theorem decomposition suggests a parallel: "recognize the discharge obligation" + "emit code that satisfies it." If you grant that decomposition, two consequences follow that are worth writing down. (i) The training signal becomes denser. A model that fails the shield can fail in two distinguishable ways — wrong obligation (the model misread the spec) or wrong execution (the model read the spec correctly but emitted incorrect code) — and these failures ought to receive different reward shapes. Current shielded-codegen literature collapses them into a single binary signal; Insight-Theorem is empirical evidence that the collapse is leaving training signal on the floor. (ii) The proposal's contribution sentence has a new candidate form. "We decompose the shielded-codegen training objective into obligation-recognition and obligation-discharge components, and show that the decomposition matches or exceeds end-to-end training under fixed compute." That's defensible, falsifiable, and lets you piggyback on Insight-Theorem's experimental methodology rather than reinventing it. Note the honest caveat the authors raise in §6 — the technique annotations require expert labor, which is why the dataset is 14k and not 140k — and the parallel caveat for your work: discharge-obligation labels also require expert (or at least specification-author) input, and your evaluation should be honest about that cost. Good paper, well worth the 25 minutes, and the decomposition idea is the load-bearing one.

⚡ Quick Reads · 15 min total
Self-Distilled RLVR (RLSD): On-Policy Distillation Meets Verifiable Rewards
arXiv · Apr 4, 2026 · on-policy distillation · teacher-traces · denser-than-RLVR signal · ~4 min
A useful complement to today's deep dive: where Insight-Theorem decomposes the training signal into recognize-then-execute, RLSD decomposes the reward source into "outcome-verifiable" (RLVR's binary signal) and "trajectory-verifiable" (the larger teacher's per-step distribution). The construction itself is unsurprising — a stronger teacher gives the student step-level signals when the verifier returns sparse outcome rewards — but the framing is the keeper. For shielded codegen, the parallel is direct: the shield gives you sound, sparse rejection; you can compose that with a separate dense acceptance signal (e.g., from a stronger code-generation model serving as teacher) without weakening the shield's soundness guarantees. Cite this paper when arguing that the shield's binary discharge signal does not need to bear the entire training-signal load.
RLVR Trending Read
KnowRL: Boosting LLM Reasoning via RL with Minimal-Sufficient Knowledge Guidance
arXiv · Apr 13, 2026 · reward-sparsity fix · hint as minimal-sufficient guidance · curriculum design · ~4 min
The diagnosis is the cleanest published statement of an issue your proposal needs to address head-on: vanilla RLVR collapses on hard problems because the reward signal is binary and the success rate is too low for the policy to bootstrap. KnowRL's fix is to inject minimal-sufficient hints — just enough domain knowledge to nudge the rollout into a region where the verifier sometimes succeeds, then taper the hint-budget as the policy improves. Two things to lift. (i) The "minimal-sufficient" framing is a more disciplined way to talk about hint design than the typical "scaffolding" or "prompt engineering" vocabulary; it has an information-theoretic flavor (you're trying to find the smallest hint that flips the success-rate cliff). (ii) The curriculum is a candidate baseline for the obligation-recognition layer of your decomposition — a system that learns to read the discharge obligation from a minimal-sufficient summary of the spec, rather than from the full spec, is operationally useful and has clean evaluation hooks.
RLVR Formal Methods Read
On Reasoning-Centric LLM-based Automated Theorem Proving
arXiv · Apr 24, 2026 · position paper · proof plans · critique loops · ATP agents · ~4 min
The position-paper companion to today's deep dive, and short enough to read in full. The argument: most LLM-ATP systems treat the LLM as a tactic generator and the proof assistant as an evaluator, leaving the LLM's reasoning capacity underused. The authors argue that proof agents should instead reason strategically — propose a plan, critique their own proposals, and revise — before emitting tactics. This is a position paper, so the contribution is the framework, not the experiments. Two takeaways for your proposal. (i) The "plan / critique / emit" three-phase decomposition maps neatly onto the obligation-recognition / obligation-discharge decomposition you'll lift from Insight-Theorem; together they suggest a four-phase pipeline (plan → recognize obligation → discharge → critique) that is more principled than what's currently in your draft. (ii) The vocabulary the paper introduces ("strategic reasoning," "self-critique," "tactic-as-output-not-as-objective") is borrowable; it's the kind of language that turns a methods paper into a position paper, which is what you eventually want §1 of your proposal to read like.
Formal Methods Agents Read
The Zig project's rationale for their firm anti-AI contribution policy — Simon Willison
simonwillison.net · Apr 30, 2026 · community governance · contribution-quality economics · palette cleanser · ~3 min
A short link-and-comment post on the Zig Software Foundation's newly-published rationale for banning LLM-assisted contributions. The argument worth keeping is not the AI-skeptic position itself but the underlying economic claim: successful open-source projects eventually receive more PRs than they can review, and the binding constraint is reviewer-time, not contribution-volume. AI-assisted PRs disproportionately consume reviewer-time relative to their accepted-contribution rate, so the optimal policy for a maintainer is to refuse them upstream rather than triage downstream. For a research-program-level read, this is a useful corrective to the tendency in verification/automation papers to treat "more output" as obviously desirable; if the bottleneck is review, automation that produces unreviewed-but-plausible artifacts can be net-negative. The shielded-codegen analogue is direct: a shield that produces "passes" without producing reviewable justifications for why it passed shifts the review burden downstream rather than reducing it. Worth three minutes for the framing.
