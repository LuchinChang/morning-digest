UCPO: Uniform-Correct Policy Optimization — Breaking RLVR’s Indifference to Diversity
arXiv · May 1, 2026·~25 min read
This is the paper that closes the loop on the week’s arc. The Binary-Rewards paper from Monday proved that the set of policies maximizing expected binary reward is infinite with no distinguished element; GCPO picked one by maximizing semantic-embedding volume; UCPO picks one by maximizing entropy conditioned on the correct set — and that, as far as RLVR is concerned, is the Jaynesian move. The construction in §3 is the part to read like a measure-theory exercise. They define the “correct support” C(x) for prompt x as the set of completions earning reward 1, and the “ideal” policy as the one that places uniform mass on C(x) and zero elsewhere — this is the maximum-entropy element of the degenerate optimum set, and it’s the maximum-entropy distribution under the constraint “put all mass on correct.” The training loss is then a conditional uniformity penalty: an additive term that pushes the policy’s conditional distribution over correct rollouts toward uniform. Three things to internalize. (1) This is the right way to think about “diversity-as-objective” in RLVR. Most prior diversity work added entropy terms over the full policy distribution — which trades off correctness against exploration in a confusing way (you’re paying entropy cost for wrong answers, which doesn’t make sense if the reward is binary). UCPO’s move is to add the entropy term only over the correct set, which decouples the two concerns: correctness still gets the binary reward, and entropy regularization gets the conditional distribution. That conditional-on-correctness formulation is the right one — once you see it you’ll wonder why it took the field this long. (2) The connection to maximum-entropy estimation in statistical mechanics is exact, not metaphorical. Jaynes’ argument was: among all distributions satisfying a set of expectation constraints, the maximum-entropy distribution is the one that makes the fewest assumptions. Here the constraint is “mass lies on C(x)” (a hard constraint, not an expectation), and the max-entropy answer is uniform on C(x). This is the limit, as β→0, of the Boltzmann distribution exp(-β·Loss)/Z restricted to C(x) — which is also exactly the construction your bookmarked “Reference-Sampled Boltzmann Projection” paper uses for KL-regularized RLVR, except that paper picks a non-uniform prior. The two papers are doing the same math with different priors; UCPO picks the uniform prior on C(x), Boltzmann Projection picks the base-model prior on C(x). Read them as a pair. (3) For your formal-methods work, this is the cleanest mathematical hook the diversity-collapse literature gives you. The question that sits unanswered — what is the right diversity functional on Lean proofs? — reduces, in the UCPO frame, to: what equivalence relation do we want uniform mass over? Two natural choices: equivalence modulo tactic permutation (gives uniform-over-distinct-tactic-traces), or equivalence modulo proof term up to αβη (gives uniform-over-essentially-distinct-proofs). The first is what current embedding-based methods approximate; the second is what you actually want and what GCPO’s embedding can’t see. UCPO with a proof-term-up-to-αβη equivalence is a real paper. Empirically the headline numbers are strong: up to +10% absolute Pass@64 on AIME24, up to 45% higher equation-level diversity within the correct set, Pass@1 competitive. The ablation in Table 3 is the one to study — their conditional-uniformity penalty alone explains most of the Pass@K gain, with the rest of the architectural choices contributing <1% each.

⚡ Quick Reads · 15 min total
GAGPO: Generalized Advantage Grouped Policy Optimization
arXiv · May 12, 2026·~5 min read
The drop-in cousin of GCPO. Where GCPO reframes credit assignment as a cooperative game and pulls in determinantal-volume machinery, GAGPO is a more conservative move: generalize GRPO’s advantage estimator with a richer group-conditioned baseline — closer in spirit to GAE (the classical Generalized Advantage Estimator from Schulman’s PPO line) than to anything cooperative-game-theoretic. The construction is λ-weighted across a stack of group-level baselines at different temporal horizons, recovering vanilla GRPO when λ=0 and approaching a full Monte Carlo estimate as λ→1. The contribution is mostly empirical: gains over GRPO at fixed compute, the bias-variance tradeoff for λ behaves like it does in vanilla GAE. Read this paper if you want a one-line patch to a GRPO codebase; read GCPO instead if you want the conceptual shift. Worth noting — the GAGPO authors are explicit that they are not trying to address diversity collapse, only variance in the advantage estimator. So this is the “same wound, different surgical instrument” member of the family: it patches the gradient-noise side of the problem, GCPO/UCPO patch the gradient-direction side.
Promoted RLVR Read
EP-GRPO: Entropy-Progress Aligned GRPO with Implicit Process Guidance
arXiv · May 7, 2026·~4 min read
Another angle on the GRPO-credit-assignment problem, this time from the process side rather than the outcome side. EP-GRPO observes three failures in vanilla GRPO: (i) uniform token-level granularity that ignores heterogeneous informational value, (ii) uniform polarity that can penalize correct steps and reward incorrect ones, and (iii) zero-variance collapse, where if every rollout in a group is correct (or every one is wrong) the GRPO advantage estimate is exactly zero and the gradient signal vanishes. The fix: use the policy’s own per-token entropy trajectory as an implicit process reward — positions where entropy drops substantially are evidence of decisive commitment, positions where it stays high are evidence of indecision. This is information-theoretically clean (entropy reduction = information gain, by definition) and it’s a self-supervised signal that doesn’t require a reward model or human labels. The (iii) failure mode — zero-variance collapse — is worth a margin note: it’s structurally the same phenomenon UCPO is trying to prevent, but on the gradient side rather than the converged-policy side. Pair this with the uPRM paper from two weeks ago — both are using self-generated signals to densify the GRPO objective without supervision.

Discovering New Theorems via LLMs with In-Context Proof Learning in Lean
arXiv · revised May 6, 2026·~3 min read
Fresh formal-methods item, and the one that lines up directly with the Direction-2 (artifact reuse) gap you flagged in yesterday’s GCPO note. The core finding is small but mechanically clean: when an LLM is searching for proofs in Lean, reusing its own previously-verified proofs as in-context examples for the next search consistently improves success rate — not because the new proofs reuse the same lemmas (they often don’t), but because the model picks up tactic-sequence patterns from its own verified output. Two things to file. (1) This is empirical evidence that the embedding-vs-tactic-permutation question from yesterday is real: the verified artifacts carry transferable structure at the tactic level, not at the lemma level. (2) The in-context-learning loop they describe is essentially a lightweight RAG over proof artifacts, with the retrieval index being “proofs we’ve already verified.” That’s the artifact-reuse story made concrete, and it’s a baseline you can beat — the natural next paper is a UCPO-flavored training objective that explicitly rewards tactic-pattern diversity within the verified set.
