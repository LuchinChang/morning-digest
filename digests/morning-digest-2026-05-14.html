🌟 Deep Dive · 25 min
FormalRewardBench: A Benchmark for Formal Theorem Proving Reward Models
arXiv · May 14, 2026·~24 min read
The paper that sits exactly on your dissertation crossbar: RLVR for theorem proving with a learned reward model instead of a binary verifier. The motivation is the sparse-credit problem that’s haunted every RLVR-for-Lean pipeline you’ve read: Lean returns 0/1, partial progress is invisible, and as soon as the problem is harder than any rollout can solve the gradient signal vanishes. The authors’ response is the natural one — train a learned reward model that scores partial proofs — and the contribution is to actually build the benchmark needed to evaluate such a thing honestly. The construction is worth reading carefully. They take 250 verified Lean 4 proofs and produce a paired incorrect variant for each, using five expert-curated error-injection strategies: forced mistakes (introduce a tactic that doesn’t close the goal), minimal single-point variations (swap one term so the proof type-checks locally but fails globally), verbose incorrect proofs (pad the proof with valid-looking but irrelevant work), natural-language justification (mix English “therefore”-style reasoning into a Lean script), and Python-code injection (a Trojan-horse test for reward models that learned to reward surface-level “code-shaped” output). The five strategies are themselves a contribution — they’re a taxonomy of the failure modes you should expect any reward model in this domain to fall for. The empirical headline is the one that should make you stop and sketch: frontier general LLMs reach 59.8% on the benchmark; specialized theorem-proving models reach 24.4%. That gap is the punchline. It says proof-writing ability is largely orthogonal to proof-evaluation ability, which means the standard pipeline (use a strong prover’s own logits as a value head) is the worst option available — it’s the configuration most likely to reward-hack itself. Three things to chase. First, the dependency on the error-injection taxonomy: the benchmark is only as good as those five strategies, and the “natural-language justification” category is the one that most resembles real LLM proof attempts — the discriminator scores there are the most informative. Second, the “Python-code injection” trap is exactly the kind of distractor you should be designing in your own evaluation harness; copy the construction. Third, the obvious next paper — a learned reward model trained on FormalRewardBench, then used as the dense signal inside a GRPO loop over a Lean prover — is the right shape for a chapter, and the benchmark removes the “but how do you evaluate it” objection that has stalled this line of work for a year. The pair-up to your May 13 deep dive (CktFormalizer) is direct: CktFormalizer is autoformalization with the verifier outside the optimizer; this paper is the prerequisite for closing that loop.

⚡ Quick Reads · 15 min total
Can RL Teach Long-Horizon Reasoning to LLMs? Expressiveness Is Key
arXiv · May 7, 2026·~5 min read (skim)
The headline result is a scaling law you should write down. The authors build ScaleLogic, a synthetic logical-reasoning environment with two independent difficulty axes — proof depth D and the expressiveness of the underlying logic (implication-only → conjunction → disjunction → negation → first-order universals). Across the axes, RL training compute follows a clean power law T ∝ Dγ with R2 > 0.99, and crucially the exponent γ rises monotonically from 1.04 in propositional logic to 2.60 in full first-order logic. The reading for your wheelhouse: this is the first paper to give you a defensible answer to “how does RL training cost scale with the formal richness of the target language?”, which is the question you’d have to answer in any FM×RL chapter. The γ−1 gap is the “extra cost of quantifiers”.

Rethinking RL for LLM Reasoning: It’s Sparse Policy Selection, Not Capability Learning
arXiv · May 7, 2026·~4 min read (skim)
The contrarian read of the week. The thesis: RL doesn’t teach the model anything new — it just redistributes probability mass across reasoning paths the base model already contains. Their evidence is token-level: across multiple model families and RL algorithms, only 1–3% of token positions are materially affected by RL fine-tuning, and the promoted token is essentially always already in the base model’s top-5. They turn this observation into ReasonMaxxer, an RL-free method that applies contrastive loss only at high-entropy decision points and matches or exceeds full RL with roughly three orders of magnitude less compute. Pair this with today’s ScaleLogic: if RL is selection, ScaleLogic’s scaling exponents are measuring how hard it is to locate the right path in the base model’s prior, not how hard it is to learn one. That reframing is worth sitting with.

Natural Policy Gradient as Doubly Smoothed Policy Iteration: A Bellman-Operator Framework
arXiv · May 2026·~3 min read (skim)
Recasts natural policy gradient (NPG) as a fixed-point iteration of a doubly smoothed Bellman operator — one smoothing from the entropy regularizer, the other from the natural-gradient preconditioner. The payoff is a distribution-free global convergence proof that doesn’t need the usual concentrability assumptions, plus a closed-form characterization of the limiting policy in terms of the Bellman operator’s spectrum. Why it matters for your stack: when you ask “what is GRPO actually converging to?”, the cleanest answer goes through NPG (since GRPO is a natural-gradient method in disguise once you account for the U-statistic structure — cf. yesterday’s Demystifying-GRPO read). Skim §3 (the operator construction) and Theorem 4.1.
Promoted RLVR Read
Using Claude Code: The Unreasonable Effectiveness of HTML
simonwillison.net · May 8, 2026·~3 min read
Short, useful, and a nice palate-cleanser before you go back to the GRPO literature. Simon’s observation: agentic coding tools quietly converged on plain HTML as their default deliverable for one-shot tools, dashboards, and report-style artifacts. Two things to take away. (1) The pragmatic reason is the same reason your digest pipeline is HTML — the format is universal, self-contained, and survives every transport channel; (2) the deeper reason is that HTML+inline-CSS is a target language with a verifier (the browser) that LLMs have seen vastly more of than any framework. That second point connects to today’s thread: HTML is to coding agents what Lean is to FormalRewardBench — the substrate the model trusts because it has a deterministic checker.
