🔬 Deep Dive · 25 min
COBALT-TLA: A Neuro-Symbolic Verification Loop for Cross-Chain Bridge Vulnerability Discovery
arXiv · Apr 12, 2026 · PROMOTED from bookmarks · TLA+ · TLC model checker · neuro-symbolic REPL · ~25 min
Read this twice — once as a vulnerability-discovery paper (the surface-level contribution) and once as a methodological manifesto (the deeper one, and the reason it's been sitting in your queue). The architecture is small enough to hold in your head: the LLM proposes a bounded TLA+ specification of a cross-chain bridge protocol; TLC, the model checker, evaluates it; the structured error trace — invariant violation, location, witness — is parsed and re-injected into the LLM's context; iterate until convergence. The claim the authors stake out, and which is what you should lift, is that this loop reframes LLM generation in a way that does not depend on the LLM being correct: it depends only on the loop's convergence, which is enforced by the deterministic semantic oracle. Hallucination becomes a transient state that the next iteration of the loop washes out, not a terminal failure mode. Three things to do with this paper. (i) Map the analogy explicitly. Their TLA+ specs are your shielded-codegen function pre/post-conditions; their TLC is your shield's discharge engine; their error traces are your shield's rejection witnesses. The mapping is one-to-one; write it out as a table in §2 of your draft. (ii) Steal the convergence framing. Their pitch is not "we made the LLM better" but "we made the loop converge"; that's a much stronger philosophical position than the one your current draft implicitly takes, and it's defensible without needing to claim anything about the LLM's internal capabilities. (iii) Note the open question they don't answer. Their convergence guarantee is empirical, not proved; for the bounded TLA+ case it's defensible because TLC is sound and the spec language is decidable, but the moment you generalize to unbounded specifications (which your proposal needs to) the argument breaks. That's the gap your theoretical contribution should fill: a convergence theorem for the shielded-codegen loop under specified conditions on the discharge structure. This is the move that turns your work from "we tried it and it works" into "we proved it converges and here's the rate" — and that's the difference between a workshop paper and a venue paper.

Verify Before You Commit: Towards Faithful Reasoning in LLM Agents via Self-Auditing
arXiv · Apr 9, 2026 · SAVeR · belief-state verification · long-horizon agents · ~4 min
The natural extension of yesterday's Faithful-Autoformalization deep dive into long-horizon agent territory: the failure mode SAVeR diagnoses is the agent that produces locally coherent reasoning at every step but accumulates an unsupported belief across steps, and the proposed defense is a self-auditor that checks each new commitment against the agent's prior belief state before allowing the action. The structural move is the same one you've been writing about — verification over internal state rather than over outputs — but pushed one level deeper: the audit is over belief consistency, not output equivalence. For your proposal, two specific lifts. (i) The belief-state framing is sharper than the language your current draft uses ("the agent's internal model") and is more compatible with formal-methods terminology; consider adopting it. (ii) The honest weakness of the construction — the auditor is itself an LLM, so the unsupported-belief failure mode could recur at the audit layer — is the same weakness AgentV-RL has, and it's the right caution to cite when you argue for a non-learned shield as a third independent layer.

From Language to Logic: Bridging LLMs & Formal Representations for RTL Assertion Generation (ProofLoop)
arXiv · Apr 24, 2026 · SystemVerilog · JasperGold · ReAct + tool use · ~4 min
The hardware-verification analogue of today's deep dive, and worth four minutes for exactly that reason: it tells you that the "solver-in-the-loop" idiom is not a quirk of the TLA+ ecosystem but a generalizable architecture. ProofLoop is a two-phase ReAct agent: Phase A gathers design context via semantic search over an AST-indexed vector DB plus structural queries to JasperGold; Phase B generates SystemVerilog Assertions and refines them using JasperGold's formal-proof feedback loop. The empirical observation worth marking — and citing as a generalizable diagnostic — is that LLMs are markedly better at fixing structural errors when given an explicit error message than at fixing temporal-logic errors, which require deeper reasoning about hardware behavior. Translated to your domain: shielded codegen will catch syntactic and structural violations cheaply but the temporal-property violations are the ones the shield will struggle with. That's a useful piece of empirical evidence to cite when you argue for a layered shield (cheap structural pass, expensive temporal pass) rather than a flat one.

A Robust Reasoning Benchmark: 14-Perturbation Pipeline for Reasoning Model Stress-Testing
arXiv · Apr 9, 2026 · perturbation testing · open-weights brittleness · IPT generalization · ~3 min
A short-but-pointed empirical contribution: the authors design a 14-technique perturbation pipeline (lexical substitution, ordering shuffle, premise reordering, distractor injection, etc.) and run it across a representative set of frontier and open-weights reasoning models. Frontier models hold up; open-weights models suffer up to a 55% accuracy drop. Two reasons this matters for your proposal. First, this is the natural empirical companion to "LLMs Gaming Verifiers" (IPT — invariance under isomorphic perturbation) — same diagnostic principle, broader perturbation set, and a wider sweep across models. Cite alongside. Second, the result reframes what "passing a benchmark" means: a shield that depends on the policy's reasoning being robust under perturbation is making a much weaker assumption when stacked on a frontier model than on an open-weights model. That asymmetry is worth acknowledging in your evaluation section.
RLVR Read Trending
The Surprising Universality of LLM Outputs: A Real-Time Verification Primitive
arXiv · Apr 27, 2026 · output-distribution overlap · model-agnostic verification · ~4 min
Three minutes for the table on page 4 and one minute for the framing. The empirical finding is that output distributions of frontier and reasonably-tuned open-weights models converge to a high degree of overlap on tasks within their joint capability — which is itself unsurprising — but the authors turn the observation into a verification primitive: an output that lies far outside the empirical convergence region is a candidate for rejection without invoking any model-specific knowledge. The honest weakness, which the paper acknowledges in §6, is that "convergence region" is defined empirically and shifts with the model population; in adversarial settings this is brittle. For your proposal, the framing is the part to lift, not the construction: there is something useful in the idea of population-level verification as a cheap pre-filter before invoking an expensive shield, even if you don't use this paper's specific instantiation. File it under "future-work pre-filter ideas" rather than core architecture.
